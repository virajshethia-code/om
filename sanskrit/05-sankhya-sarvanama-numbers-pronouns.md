# Sankhya & Sarvanama — Numbers and Pronouns

## The Setup

You now know that Sanskrit nouns carry their role (vibhakti), verbs carry their tense and person (lakara), and sounds merge at boundaries (sandhi). Two critical word classes remain before you can construct real sentences: **numbers** and **pronouns**.

Both behave unlike anything in English. Numbers decline through cases just like nouns. Pronouns encode gender, number, AND case in a single word — no helper words, no word-order dependency. One form does the work of three English words.

**सङ्ख्या** (saṅkhyā) = **सम्** (sam, together) + **ख्या** (khyā, to tell/enumerate) → "complete enumeration"

**सर्वनाम** (sarvanāma) = **सर्व** (sarva, all) + **नाम** (nāma, name) → "name for all" — a word that stands in for any noun

## Part II: Numbers Decline — They Take Vibhakti

This is where Sanskrit numbers get interesting. In English, "two" is always "two." In Sanskrit, numbers 1–4 decline for **gender and case**, just like adjectives. Numbers 5–10 decline for case but not gender.

### एक (eka, one) — Declines Like a Pronoun

एक takes all three genders and all eight cases, but only exists in singular (obviously).

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | एकः (ekaḥ) | एका (ekā) | एकम् (ekam) |
| Dvitiya (2) | एकम् (ekam) | एकाम् (ekām) | एकम् (ekam) |
| Tritiya (3) | एकेन (ekena) | एकया (ekayā) | एकेन (ekena) |
| Chaturthi (4) | एकस्मै (ekasmai) | एकस्यै (ekasyai) | एकस्मै (ekasmai) |
| Panchami (5) | एकस्मात् (ekasmāt) | एकस्याः (ekasyāḥ) | एकस्मात् (ekasmāt) |
| Shashthi (6) | एकस्य (ekasya) | एकस्याः (ekasyāḥ) | एकस्य (ekasya) |
| Saptami (7) | एकस्मिन् (ekasmin) | एकस्याम् (ekasyām) | एकस्मिन् (ekasmin) |

> Notice: एक doesn't follow the regular अ-stem (rāma-type) declension. It follows the **pronominal declension** — the same pattern as सर्व, तत्, etc. This is your first hint that numbers and pronouns are deeply related in Sanskrit grammar.

### द्वि (dvi, two) — Only Dual Forms

द्वि exists only in the dual number (द्विवचन). It still takes three genders.

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | द्वौ (dvau) | द्वे (dve) | द्वे (dve) |
| Dvitiya (2) | द्वौ (dvau) | द्वे (dve) | द्वे (dve) |
| Tritiya (3) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) |
| Chaturthi (4) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) |
| Panchami (5) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) | द्वाभ्याम् (dvābhyām) |
| Shashthi (6) | द्वयोः (dvayoḥ) | द्वयोः (dvayoḥ) | द्वयोः (dvayoḥ) |
| Saptami (7) | द्वयोः (dvayoḥ) | द्वयोः (dvayoḥ) | द्वयोः (dvayoḥ) |

> Cases 3, 4, and 5 merge into one form in the dual. Cases 6 and 7 merge too. The dual number compresses the case system.

### Numbers 5–10: Case But No Gender

From पञ्चन् (5) onward, numbers decline for case but are gender-neutral. They always take **plural** forms.

**पञ्चन् (pañcan, five):**

| Case | Form |
|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | पञ्च (pañca) |
| Dvitiya (2) | पञ्च (pañca) |
| Tritiya (3) | पञ्चभिः (pañcabhiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | पञ्चभ्यः (pañcabhyaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | पञ्चभ्यः (pañcabhyaḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | पञ्चानाम् (pañcānām) |
| Saptami (7) | पञ्चसु (pañcasu) |

The other numbers (6–10) follow the same pattern with their respective stems.

### Numbers In Action

```
पञ्च बालाः क्रीडन्ति
pañca bālāḥ krīḍanti
five boys play
```

Here **पञ्च** is in prathama vibhakti (nominative) because it describes the subject **बालाः**.

```
त्रिषु ग्रामेषु
triṣu grāmeṣu
in three villages
```

Both the number and the noun take saptami vibhakti (locative). The number agrees with the noun in case.

## Part III: Personal Pronouns (Purushavachaka Sarvanama)

### First Person — अस्मद् (asmad)

The stem is **अस्मद्** (asmad), but you'll never see it bare. The declined forms are **suppletive** — they don't follow regular rules. You just learn them.

| Case | Singular (I) | Dual (we two) | Plural (we) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **अहम्** (aham) | **आवाम्** (āvām) | **वयम्** (vayam) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **माम्** / **मा** (mām / mā) | **आवाम्** / **नौ** (āvām / nau) | **अस्मान्** / **नः** (asmān / naḥ) |
| Tritiya (3) | **मया** (mayā) | **आवाभ्याम्** (āvābhyām) | **अस्माभिः** (asmābhiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **मह्यम्** / **मे** (mahyam / me) | **आवाभ्याम्** / **नौ** (āvābhyām / nau) | **अस्मभ्यम्** / **नः** (asmabhyam / naḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **मत्** (mat) | **आवाभ्याम्** (āvābhyām) | **अस्मत्** (asmat) |
| Shashthi (6) | **मम** / **मे** (mama / me) | **आवयोः** / **नौ** (āvayoḥ / nau) | **अस्माकम्** / **नः** (asmākam / naḥ) |
| Saptami (7) | **मयि** (mayi) | **आवयोः** (āvayoḥ) | **अस्मासु** (asmāsu) |

> The short forms (**मा, मे, नौ, नः**) are **enclitic** — they attach to the preceding word and never start a sentence. They're like the unstressed "me" in English "give me that" versus the stressed "ME" in "give it to ME."

> **अहम्** (aham) → Latin "ego" → English "I". Same PIE root *éǵh₂. The first person pronoun has survived for 5,000+ years.

### Second Person — युष्मद् (yuṣmad)

| Case | Singular (you) | Dual (you two) | Plural (you all) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **त्वम्** (tvam) | **युवाम्** (yuvām) | **यूयम्** (yūyam) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **त्वाम्** / **त्वा** (tvām / tvā) | **युवाम्** / **वाम्** (yuvām / vām) | **युष्मान्** / **वः** (yuṣmān / vaḥ) |
| Tritiya (3) | **त्वया** (tvayā) | **युवाभ्याम्** (yuvābhyām) | **युष्माभिः** (yuṣmābhiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **तुभ्यम्** / **ते** (tubhyam / te) | **युवाभ्याम्** / **वाम्** (yuvābhyām / vām) | **युष्मभ्यम्** / **वः** (yuṣmabhyam / vaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **त्वत्** (tvat) | **युवाभ्याम्** (yuvābhyām) | **युष्मत्** (yuṣmat) |
| Shashthi (6) | **तव** / **ते** (tava / te) | **युवयोः** / **वाम्** (yuvayoḥ / vām) | **युष्माकम्** / **वः** (yuṣmākam / vaḥ) |
| Saptami (7) | **त्वयि** (tvayi) | **युवयोः** (yuvayoḥ) | **युष्मासु** (yuṣmāsu) |

> **Key pattern**: first and second person pronouns have **no gender**. "I" and "you" don't need to declare masculine/feminine/neuter — the speaker and listener are known. Gender enters only in the **third person**, where you're pointing at someone/something absent.

## Part IV: Third Person / Demonstrative — तत् (tat, that)

The pronoun **तत्** (tat) is the workhorse of Sanskrit. It means "that" as a demonstrative, and serves as "he/she/it/they" as a third-person pronoun. There is no separate word for "he" — **सः** (saḥ) literally means "that one (masculine)."

### Masculine — तत् (tat)

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **सः** (saḥ) | **तौ** (tau) | **ते** (te) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **तम्** (tam) | **तौ** (tau) | **तान्** (tān) |
| Tritiya (3) | **तेन** (tena) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **तैः** (taiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **तस्मै** (tasmai) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **तेभ्यः** (tebhyaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **तस्मात्** (tasmāt) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **तेभ्यः** (tebhyaḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | **तस्य** (tasya) | **तयोः** (tayoḥ) | **तेषाम्** (teṣām) |
| Saptami (7) | **तस्मिन्** (tasmin) | **तयोः** (tayoḥ) | **तेषु** (teṣu) |

> **तस्मात्** (tasmāt, "from that / therefore") — you've seen this word. It appears constantly in the Gita and Upanishads as a logical connector: "therefore." It's just the ablative of तत्.

### Feminine — तत् (tat)

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **सा** (sā) | **ते** (te) | **ताः** (tāḥ) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **ताम्** (tām) | **ते** (te) | **ताः** (tāḥ) |
| Tritiya (3) | **तया** (tayā) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **ताभिः** (tābhiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **तस्यै** (tasyai) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **ताभ्यः** (tābhyaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **तस्याः** (tasyāḥ) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **ताभ्यः** (tābhyaḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | **तस्याः** (tasyāḥ) | **तयोः** (tayoḥ) | **तासाम्** (tāsām) |
| Saptami (7) | **तस्याम्** (tasyām) | **तयोः** (tayoḥ) | **तासु** (tāsu) |

### Neuter — तत् (tat)

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **तत्** (tat) | **ते** (te) | **तानि** (tāni) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **तत्** (tat) | **ते** (te) | **तानि** (tāni) |
| Tritiya (3) | **तेन** (tena) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **तैः** (taiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **तस्मै** (tasmai) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **तेभ्यः** (tebhyaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **तस्मात्** (tasmāt) | **ताभ्याम्** (tābhyām) | **तेभ्यः** (tebhyaḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | **तस्य** (tasya) | **तयोः** (tayoḥ) | **तेषाम्** (teṣām) |
| Saptami (7) | **तस्मिन्** (tasmin) | **तयोः** (tayoḥ) | **तेषु** (teṣu) |

> Neuter differs from masculine only in cases 1 and 2. Cases 3–7 are identical. This holds across most Sanskrit declensions — the neuter "splits off" only in the nominative and accusative.

## Part V: Demonstrative Pronouns — This vs That

Sanskrit distinguishes distance in reference, like "this" vs "that" in English, but with more precision.

### एतत् (etat) — "this" (near the speaker)

**एतत्** = **ए** (e) + **तत्** (tat) → adds a prefix to the तत् forms.

The declension of एतत् follows तत् exactly, with **ए** prefixed:

| | Masculine Sg. | Feminine Sg. | Neuter Sg. |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **एषः** (eṣaḥ) | **एषा** (eṣā) | **एतत्** (etat) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **एतम्** (etam) | **एताम्** (etām) | **एतत्** (etat) |
| Tritiya (3) | **एतेन** (etena) | **एतया** (etayā) | **एतेन** (etena) |
| Shashthi (6) | **एतस्य** (etasya) | **एतस्याः** (etasyāḥ) | **एतस्य** (etasya) |

The rest follows the same prefix pattern. If you know तत्, you know एतत्.

### इदम् (idam) — "this" (general proximity)

इदम् is the other "this" pronoun. It's more common and more irregular.

**Masculine:**

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **अयम्** (ayam) | **इमौ** (imau) | **इमे** (ime) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **इमम्** (imam) | **इमौ** (imau) | **इमान्** (imān) |
| Tritiya (3) | **अनेन** (anena) | **आभ्याम्** (ābhyām) | **एभिः** (ebhiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **अस्मै** (asmai) | **आभ्याम्** (ābhyām) | **एभ्यः** (ebhyaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **अस्मात्** (asmāt) | **आभ्याम्** (ābhyām) | **एभ्यः** (ebhyaḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | **अस्य** (asya) | **अनयोः** (anayoḥ) | **एषाम्** (eṣām) |
| Saptami (7) | **अस्मिन्** (asmin) | **अनयोः** (anayoḥ) | **एषु** (eṣu) |

**Feminine:**

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **इयम्** (iyam) | **इमे** (ime) | **इमाः** (imāḥ) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **इमाम्** (imām) | **इमे** (ime) | **इमाः** (imāḥ) |
| Tritiya (3) | **अनया** (anayā) | **आभ्याम्** (ābhyām) | **आभिः** (ābhiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **अस्यै** (asyai) | **आभ्याम्** (ābhyām) | **आभ्यः** (ābhyaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **अस्याः** (asyāḥ) | **आभ्याम्** (ābhyām) | **आभ्यः** (ābhyaḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | **अस्याः** (asyāḥ) | **अनयोः** (anayoḥ) | **आसाम्** (āsām) |
| Saptami (7) | **अस्याम्** (asyām) | **अनयोः** (anayoḥ) | **आसु** (āsu) |

**Neuter:**

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **इदम्** (idam) | **इमे** (ime) | **इमानि** (imāni) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **इदम्** (idam) | **इमे** (ime) | **इमानि** (imāni) |

Cases 3–7 neuter = same as masculine (same pattern as तत्).

### The Three-Way System

| Pronoun | Meaning | Distance |
|---|---|---|
| **इदम्** (idam) | this | Near speaker — what I'm holding, what's here |
| **एतत्** (etat) | this | Near speaker — slightly more emphatic/specific |
| **तत्** (tat) | that | Away from speaker — what's over there, what was mentioned |

> Compare: English has two (this/that). Sanskrit has three. Japanese has three too (kore/sore/are), split by speaker-listener-neither. Sanskrit's split is subtler — इदम् vs एतत् is about emphasis, not spatial distance.

## Part VI: Interrogative Pronoun — किम् (kim, who/what)

**किम्** (kim) = the question-word root. It asks "who?" for people and "what?" for things — same pronoun, different gender.

### Masculine — कः (kaḥ, who?)

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **कः** (kaḥ) | **कौ** (kau) | **के** (ke) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **कम्** (kam) | **कौ** (kau) | **कान्** (kān) |
| Tritiya (3) | **केन** (kena) | **काभ्याम्** (kābhyām) | **कैः** (kaiḥ) |
| Chaturthi (4) | **कस्मै** (kasmai) | **काभ्याम्** (kābhyām) | **केभ्यः** (kebhyaḥ) |
| Panchami (5) | **कस्मात्** (kasmāt) | **काभ्याम्** (kābhyām) | **केभ्यः** (kebhyaḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | **कस्य** (kasya) | **कयोः** (kayoḥ) | **केषाम्** (keṣām) |
| Saptami (7) | **कस्मिन्** (kasmin) | **कयोः** (kayoḥ) | **केषु** (keṣu) |

### Feminine — का (kā, who?/which?)

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **का** (kā) | **के** (ke) | **काः** (kāḥ) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **काम्** (kām) | **के** (ke) | **काः** (kāḥ) |
| Tritiya (3) | **कया** (kayā) | **काभ्याम्** (kābhyām) | **काभिः** (kābhiḥ) |
| Shashthi (6) | **कस्याः** (kasyāḥ) | **कयोः** (kayoḥ) | **कासाम्** (kāsām) |

### Neuter — किम् (kim, what?)

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prathama (1) | **किम्** (kim) | **के** (ke) | **कानि** (kāni) |
| Dvitiya (2) | **किम्** (kim) | **के** (ke) | **कानि** (kāni) |

Cases 3–7 neuter = same as masculine.

> **Pattern alert**: compare तत् and किम् side by side. Replace **त** with **क** in nearly every form: तस्मै → कस्मै, तेन → केन, तस्य → कस्य. The demonstrative and interrogative are structural mirrors. Panini would call this predictable — the pronominal declension is a single template, swapping stems.

### किम् Compounds — Building Question Words

किम् is also a building block:

| Compound | Breakdown | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **किमर्थम्** (kimartham) | किम् + अर्थम् | for what purpose? / why? |
| **कुत्र** (kutra) | कु (from किम्) + त्र | where? |
| **कदा** (kadā) | क + दा | when? |
| **कथम्** (katham) | क + थम् | how? |
| **किमपि** (kimapi) | किम् + अपि | something (indefinite) |

> Add **अपि** (api) to any किम्-form and it becomes indefinite: कः (who?) → कोऽपि (koʼpi, someone). Add **न** and it becomes negative: न कोऽपि (na koʼpi, no one). A single system generates questions, indefinites, and negatives.

## Part VII: Pronouns Encode More Than English

Consider the English sentence: "Give it to her."

- "it" — no case marking (object only by position), no gender, no number distinction
- "her" — encodes gender (feminine) and case (dative), but not number

Now the Sanskrit equivalent:

```
तत् तस्यै देहि
tat tasyai dehi
that(neuter/accusative/singular) her(feminine/dative/singular) give(imperative/2nd-person/singular)
```

Every word carries its full identity:

| Word | Encodes |
|---|---|
| **तत्** (tat) | neuter gender + accusative case + singular number |
| **तस्यै** (tasyai) | feminine gender + dative case + singular number |
| **देहि** (dehi) | imperative mood + second person + singular number |

Three words. Nine pieces of information. No word-order dependency. You could rearrange them any way and the meaning wouldn't change:

```
तस्यै तत् देहि = देहि तत् तस्यै = तत् देहि तस्यै
```

All identical in meaning.

In English, you need word order + prepositions + context to recover the same information. In Sanskrit, the morphology does the work.

### The Trade-Off

| Feature | English | Sanskrit |
|---|---|---|
| Forms to memorize | Few (~5 pronoun forms per person) | Many (~21 forms per pronoun per gender) |
| Word order | Fixed (SVO) — meaning depends on position | Free — meaning is in the morphology |
| Ambiguity | Common ("They saw her duck" — ?) | Rare — each form is explicit |
| Compression | Low — needs helper words (to, for, by, of) | High — one word = gender + case + number |

## Part VIII: Practice Sentences

### Reading

Parse each sentence — identify the pronoun form, its case, gender, and number:

**1.**
> अहं पुस्तकं पठामि
> ahaṃ pustakaṃ paṭhāmi
> I book read
> "I read a book."

- अहम् → prathama, singular, first person (the doer)
- पुस्तकम् → dvitiya, neuter, singular (the thing read)
- पठामि → present tense, first person, singular

**2.**
> सः तस्यै पुष्पं ददाति
> saḥ tasyai puṣpaṃ dadāti
> he to-her flower gives
> "He gives a flower to her."

- सः → prathama, masculine, singular (he — the doer)
- तस्यै → chaturthi, feminine, singular (to/for her — the recipient)
- पुष्पम् → dvitiya, neuter, singular (the flower — the thing given)
- ददाति → present tense, third person, singular

**3.**
> के त्वां पृच्छन्ति?
> ke tvāṃ pṛcchanti?
> who(pl) you ask?
> "Who (all) are asking you?"

- के → prathama, masculine, plural (who — the doers, plural)
- त्वाम् → dvitiya, singular, second person (you — the one asked)
- पृच्छन्ति → present tense, third person, plural

**4.**
> एषां पञ्चानां बालानां मध्ये अयं श्रेष्ठः
> eṣāṃ pañcānāṃ bālānāṃ madhye ayaṃ śreṣṭhaḥ
> of-these five boys among this-one best
> "Among these five boys, this one is the best."

- एषाम् → shashthi, masculine, plural (of these)
- पञ्चानाम् → shashthi, plural (of five) — number declines in same case as noun!
- बालानाम् → shashthi, masculine, plural (of boys)
- मध्ये → saptami (locative — "in the middle / among")
- अयम् → prathama, masculine, singular (this one — the subject)
- श्रेष्ठः → prathama, masculine, singular (best — adjective agrees with subject)

**5.**
> किं तव नाम?
> kiṃ tava nāma?
> what your name?
> "What is your name?"

- किम् → prathama, neuter, singular (what)
- तव → shashthi, singular, second person (your)
- नाम → prathama, neuter, singular (name)
