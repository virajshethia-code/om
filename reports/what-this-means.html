One Brain For Everything

The same AI that writes your poem also does your tax logic. It doesn't switch modes. It doesn't know the difference.

🤥

Confident Hallucinations

Real Outcomes for Real People

Here's what actually changes if this works.

⚖

A Lawyer Reviewing a Contract

Uses: Mimamsa (text interpretation) + Nyaya (logic)

Instead of asking AI "review this contract" and getting a vague summary, Mimamsa extracts every obligation as a specific command: who must do what, by when, under what conditions. Nyaya then tests each clause for internal contradictions.

Input: 200-page vendor agreement
Mimamsa output: 47 vidhis (obligations), 12 nishedhas (prohibitions), 8 ambiguous clauses flagged for shadlinga resolution
Nyaya output: Clause 14.3 contradicts clause 7.1 — the indemnity cap conflicts with the liability clause. Fallacy: viruddha (self-contradiction).
🏗

A Project Manager Planning a Crane Lift

Uses: Samkhya (decomposition) + Vaisheshika (root cause)

Samkhya enumerates every component of the lift — not just the obvious ones (load, boom, radius) but the hidden ones nobody lists until something goes wrong (ground bearing pressure, wind gradient between ground and boom tip, rigging geometry).

Input: "Plan a 40-ton lift with a 200-ton crawler crane on soft ground"
Samkhya output: 23 components enumerated including 4 non-obvious: subsurface drainage affecting mat bearing capacity, asymmetric outrigger loading during swing, boom deflection at 85% chart capacity, ground slope creating moment arm
Vaisheshika: The irreducible atom of risk is ground bearing — if it fails, nothing else matters. Test it first.
📊

A Startup CEO Making a Pivot Decision

Uses: Yaksha Protocol (all engines debate)

Instead of one AI opinion, the Yaksha Protocol runs three reasoning engines in parallel and synthesizes. Nyaya checks if the logic for pivoting holds. Samkhya maps what actually changes. Yoga cuts through the noise to find the one thing that matters. Then Vedanta resolves the apparent contradiction between "our product works" and "growth has stalled."

Input: "Should we pivot from B2B to B2C?"
Consensus: All engines agree the product-market fit data is insufficient for B2C
Tension: Nyaya says the pivot argument is logically weak. Yoga says the real question isn't B2B vs B2C — it's "why has growth stalled?"
Vedanta synthesis: The pivot is a solution to the wrong problem. The growth stall is a distribution issue, not a market issue. Fixing distribution in B2B is cheaper and lower risk than rebuilding for B2C.
🛡

A Developer Debugging a Production Issue

Uses: Vaisheshika (atomic decomposition) + Yoga (noise filtering)

The error log is 500 lines. Yoga strips the noise first — 470 lines are routine. 30 lines matter. Vaisheshika decomposes the 30 lines to find the irreducible atom: a race condition between two services sharing a cache key.

Input: 500-line error log + system architecture description
Yoga: Signal score: 6% of log is relevant. Core issue in lines 203-209 and 441-447.
Vaisheshika: Irreducible atom: service A writes cache key X, service B reads it 2ms later before the write is consistent. The atom is the 2ms gap. Fix: read-after-write consistency on the cache, or version the key.
🎓

A Student Writing a Thesis

Uses: Vedanta (contradiction resolution) + Vritti Filter (quality check)

The student has 20 sources that seem to contradict each other. Vedanta doesn't pick a side — it finds the deeper principle that explains why both sides have evidence. Then the Vritti Filter checks the student's draft: "Paragraph 3 is vikalpa — it sounds analytical but says nothing falsifiable. Paragraph 7 is smriti — you're recalling a textbook definition, not arguing. Paragraph 12 is pramana — this is your genuine contribution."

Vedanta: Sources A-L say remote work increases productivity. Sources M-T say it decreases it. Both are measuring different things — A-L measure output quantity, M-T measure collaborative innovation. The reframing: remote work increases individual output and decreases collective invention. Both are true. The thesis question is wrong.
Vritti Filter on draft: 4 paragraphs pramana (genuine argument), 6 paragraphs smriti (recalled), 2 paragraphs vikalpa (empty hedging). Suggested: rewrite the 2 vikalpa paragraphs or cut them.

Anyone Who Pays for AI API Calls

Uses: Shakti (compute management) + Guna (mode selection)

Right now, every AI call uses the most expensive model. Shakti routes simple lookups to a cheap model (Haiku — $0.25/M tokens), balanced reasoning to a mid model (Sonnet — $3/M), and only uses the expensive model (Opus — $15/M) for genuinely hard problems. Caches repeated questions. Result: same quality, 60-70% less spend.

Before Shakti: 100 queries/day × Opus = ~$15/day = $450/month
After Shakti: 60 queries routed to Haiku, 30 to Sonnet, 10 to Opus = ~$4.50/day = $135/month
Savings: $315/month with no quality loss on the queries that matter

Complex problems need multiple ways of thinking, applied in the right order.
