Project VAYU

Autonomous Crane Programme — Heft Energy & Heavy Equipment

"vayu" — the wind god, force that moves. From windmill sites to autonomous lift.

Executive Summary Board View

Objective: Build India's first autonomous crane capability, starting with a single lift cycle on windmill erection sites, using Heft's existing Sany STC 1600 (160T) fleet.

First Target: Autonomous loading/unloading of turbine components (tower sections, blades, nacelles) from transport trucks to staging areas on wind farm sites.

Why this lift: Ground-to-ground, single crane, predictable loads, 300+ repetitions per 50-turbine site, lowest consequence of failure. If the system makes a mistake, a tower section sits down hard. Nobody falls from 80 metres.

Approach: Retrofit one STC 1600 with sensors + controls. Use Claude AI as the primary research and algorithm design engine, with 2 in-house controls engineers for physical integration. Lightweight IIT advisory for regulatory credibility. Prove safety with 1,000 supervised lifts before any unsupervised operation.

Strategic Decisions Locked

Decision 1 — Crane Model

Sany STC 1600 (160T truck crane) — High unit count in Heft fleet, primary workhorse on windmill erection sites. Sany provides accessible technical documentation and parts supply chain.

Decision 2 — First Lift Cycle

Loading/unloading turbine components at wind farm sites — Tower sections (cylinders, known weight), blades (long, standardised), nacelles (heavy, consistent). Pick from truck, place in staging area. Ground-to-ground, single crane, 300+ reps per project.

Decision 3 — Controls Approach

Claude AI + Internal Engineers + IIT Advisory. Claude handles all research, algorithm design, simulation, and software architecture — faster and cheaper than a full lab partnership. Two in-house controls engineers handle physical integration, on-crane testing, and hardware tuning. A lightweight IIT advisory relationship (1 professor, quarterly reviews) provides regulatory credibility and a safety net for edge cases in nonlinear dynamics. Robotics startup acquisition remains a future option. Full IP ownership with Heft from day one.

1.2 — Claude AI: Research & Algorithm Design Sprint (Month 0–4)

Use Claude as the primary research engine to do what an IIT lab would take 6-12 months to deliver — in weeks:

  • Literature review: Exhaustive survey of anti-sway control algorithms (MPC, input shaping, sliding-mode control, energy-based methods). Claude reviews 100+ papers, extracts the algorithms, identifies which are proven on similar crane types.
  • Algorithm design: Claude designs the anti-sway controller architecture — model-predictive control with wind disturbance feedforward, tuned for the STC 1600's boom geometry and hydraulic response characteristics.
  • Simulation environment: Claude builds a full Python/MATLAB simulation of the STC 1600 — pendulum dynamics, cable length effects, wind disturbance, hydraulic actuator delays. Validated against Phase 1 sensor data.
  • Lift planner: Claude architects and codes the path planning software — pick/place optimisation within load chart constraints.
  • Safety case drafting: Claude drafts the IEC 61508 safety case structure, hazard analysis, and fault trees. Human safety engineer reviews and signs off.
  • Budget: Claude Code subscriptions + compute — ₹2,00,000 — ₹4,00,000/year

1.3 — IIT Advisory Setup (Month 3)

Establish a lightweight advisory relationship with IIT Bombay or IIT Madras — NOT a full research partnership:

  • 1 professor as technical advisor, quarterly reviews of control system design
  • Professor validates Claude's algorithm choices and simulation models
  • IIT name on the safety validation report — critical for regulatory credibility
  • Option to hire graduating students who are familiar with the project
  • Budget: ₹10,00,000 — ₹15,00,000/year (advisory fee + student stipends)
  • 100% IP remains with Heft — advisory, not joint research

1.4 — Data Collection on Live Sites (Month 3–8)

Deploy the instrumented crane to 2-3 windmill erection sites. Record every loading/unloading cycle with full sensor data + operator joystick inputs. Target: 500+ recorded lift cycles.

  • Data captured per lift: sensor streams, operator control inputs (joystick position, timing), load weight, wind conditions, cycle time, any near-miss or correction
  • This data becomes the training set for the planning system and the baseline for proving autonomous lifts are at least as safe as human lifts
  • Assign one site engineer to tag each lift with metadata (load type, weight, pick/place coordinates, weather, any anomaly)

1.5 — Hire Safety Engineer (Month 2)

Hire one functional safety engineer with IEC 61508 / ISO 13849 experience. This person writes the safety case BEFORE any control software is written. The safety case defines what the software must guarantee — not the other way around.

Phase 1 Gate — Month 8
Pass criteria: 500+ lift cycles recorded with full sensor data. Claude-designed anti-sway controller running in simulation. IIT advisory engaged. Safety engineer on board. First 2 controls engineers hired. Baseline metrics established (average cycle time, variance, near-miss rate with human operators).

Phase 2: Prototype Month 6–15

2
Build the Brain
Duration: 9 months (overlaps Phase 1)  |  Investment: ₹2,50,00,000 — ₹3,50,00,000

2.1 — Electro-Hydraulic Retrofit (Month 6–10)

This is the mechanical heart of the project. Replace the STC 1600's manual hydraulic valve bank with software-commandable electro-hydraulic valves. This lets software control boom raise/lower, telescope, swing, and hoist.

  • Work with Sany or a hydraulic systems integrator (e.g., Bosch Rexroth, Parker Hannifin)
  • Retain full manual override — operator can grab controls at any time and the system yields instantly
  • Budget: ₹40,00,000 — ₹60,00,000 for the electro-hydraulic conversion
  • HIGH RISK This is the hardest retrofit step. The crane was not designed for this. Expect iteration.

2.2 — Anti-Sway Controller (Month 6–14)

Claude designs the algorithm. Your controls engineers implement and tune it on the physical crane. IIT advisor reviews quarterly. The controller must handle:

  • Pendulum suppression during hoist, swing, and telescope motions
  • Wind disturbance compensation (the data from Phase 1 feeds the wind model)
  • Variable cable length (sway dynamics change as cable gets shorter/longer)
  • Approach: model-predictive control (MPC) or input shaping — Claude evaluates both against simulation data, IIT advisor validates the choice
  • Claude delivers: algorithm design, simulation code, parameter tuning in simulation, documentation
  • Engineers deliver: real-time implementation on embedded hardware, physical tuning on the crane, handling sensor noise and hydraulic backlash that simulation doesn't capture
  • IIT advisor delivers: quarterly review of controller stability proofs, edge case identification from nonlinear dynamics expertise

2.3 — Lift Planner (Month 8–14)

Software that takes a pick point and place point and computes the optimal crane motion sequence:

  • Input: 3D site model, pick coordinates, place coordinates, load weight, crane configuration, wind
  • Output: motion profile for each axis (hoist, swing, telescope, boom) with smooth acceleration curves
  • Constraint: stay within load chart at all times, maintain stability margin of at least 15%
  • Use recorded human lift data (Phase 1) as the reference — the planner should produce paths at least as smooth as the best human operators

2.4 — Safety Interlock System (Month 8–12)

Hardware-level safety stops that cannot be overridden by software:

InterlockTriggerAction
OverloadLoad exceeds 85% of chart capacity at current radiusBlock hoist up, allow hoist down only
Two-blockHook block approaches boom tip beyond safe distanceStop hoist up immediately
Wind limitWind exceeds limit for current load/radiusPause all motion, alert supervisor
TiltCrane chassis tilt exceeds 1.5°Emergency stop all motion
Exclusion zoneBoom tip enters geo-fenced prohibited areaBlock motion into zone
Human detectionPerson detected in swing/load pathPause motion, alert
Outrigger pressureAny outrigger exceeds ground bearing limitStop all motion

NON-NEGOTIABLE These interlocks are implemented in a separate hardware safety PLC, not in the main computer. If the main computer crashes, the interlocks still work.

3.1 — Controlled Test Environment (Month 14–16)

Set up a test area — ideally at a Heft yard or a quiet section of a wind farm site. Place known loads (concrete blocks matching tower section weight/shape) at defined pick/place points. No live construction activity nearby.

  • First 50 lifts: system plans the lift, operator reviews and approves each step before execution
  • Next 50 lifts: system executes continuously, operator watches with hand on override
  • Every single lift logged: sensor data, planned path vs actual path, any sway, any interlock trigger

3.3 — Live Site Integration (Month 20–23)

Deploy to an active windmill erection site. The autonomous system handles loading/unloading while the main erection crane (600T+ crawler) does the turbine assembly with human operators.

  • Operator remains in the cab for every autonomous lift
  • Remote operations console set up in the site office — secondary monitoring
  • Site workers briefed on autonomous operations, geo-fenced exclusion zones active
  • Target: 100 lifts on a live site with zero incidents
Phase 3 Gate — Month 23
Pass criteria: 200+ autonomous lifts completed (test + live site). Zero safety incidents. Cycle time within 120% of best human operators. Anti-sway performance measurably better than human average. All data logged and reviewed.

4.2 — Safety Case Submission (Month 25–28)

Compile all 1,000-lift data into a formal safety case document per IEC 61508. This becomes the basis for regulatory certification. Include:

  • Hazard analysis (every way the system can fail, and what prevents each)
  • Fault tree analysis (probability of each failure mode)
  • Operational data (1,000 lifts, zero incidents, all interlock activations analysed)
  • Comparison to human operator baseline (from Phase 1 data)

4.3 — Insurance Framework (Month 26–30)

Work with Heft's insurance provider to establish coverage for autonomous crane operations. The 1,000-lift data is your negotiating tool. Potential for lower premiums if the data shows autonomous lifts are safer than human-operated ones (fewer near-misses, more consistent, no fatigue-related errors).

Phase 4 Gate — Month 30
Pass criteria: 1,000 lifts with zero safety incidents. Safety case accepted by DGIW or relevant authority (IIT advisory name on validation report). Insurance coverage secured. Internal team + Claude fully capable of maintaining and extending the system independently.

The Darshana View Why This Plan

This plan was designed by running the question through all six Darshana reasoning engines of the OM architecture:

DarshanaWhat It RevealedHow It Shaped the Plan
Nyaya (Logic) The mining truck analogy breaks at suspended dynamic loads. Crane automation is a different physics problem. Anti-sway controller is the critical technical deliverable, not AI/ML. Claude designs the algorithms; in-house engineers tune on real hardware. IIT advisor validates.
Samkhya (Enumeration) 28 components needed. The "rigging intelligence" gap — nobody in autonomy research models the rigging. Phase 3 starts with simplest rigging (tower section slings) and defers complex rigging (blade yokes) to later.
Yoga (Focus) Safety is the one factor that determines everything. One incident kills the programme. Hardware interlocks as independent safety layer. 1,000-lift milestone before any commercial claim. Safety case written BEFORE software.
Vedanta (Unity) The real unit is the decision point, not the crane. Redistribute decisions between human and machine. Operator stays as supervisor. Autonomy handles the boring decisions (smooth motion) while human handles exceptions.
Mimamsa (Action) 7 executable commands with strict dependencies. The critical path is: data → safety case → software → proof. Phase gates with clear pass/fail criteria. No phase starts until the previous gate is passed.
Vaisheshika (Atoms) Three irreducible components: load physics model, safety interlocks, operational envelope restriction. These three are the non-negotiable deliverables. Everything else can be swapped or upgraded.
"Don't automate the crane. Automate the lift cycle. Don't replace the operator. Redistribute the decisions. Don't prove the technology works. Prove 1,000 lifts are safe. The crane that does less but guarantees it — wins."
