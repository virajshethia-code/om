# Yoga — Discipline of Mind

## Yoga as a Darshana

Yoga is one of the six orthodox (आस्तिक, āstika) darshanas of Hindu philosophy. It is traditionally paired with Samkhya in what is perhaps the most elegant division of labor in the history of thought: **Samkhya provides the theory, Yoga provides the practice.**

| Aspect | **सांख्य (Sāṃkhya)** | **योग (Yoga)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Mode** | Theoretical — enumeration and analysis | Practical — method and discipline |
| **Question** | "What is the structure of reality?" | "How do you realize that structure experientially?" |
| **Key text** | Samkhya Karika (Ishvarakrishna) | Yoga Sutras (Patanjali) |
| **God** | निरीश्वर (nirīśvara) — no God postulated | सेश्वर (seśvara) — Ishvara introduced as a special Purusha |
| **25 Tattvas** | The core framework | Accepted wholesale |
| **Liberation** | Through discriminative knowledge (viveka) | Through systematic mind-discipline (citta-vritti-nirodha) |
| **Method** | Analysis — you *think* your way to distinguishing Purusha from Prakriti | Practice — you *train* your way to that distinction through progressive stages |

They are two faces of the same coin. Samkhya tells you the map. Yoga teaches you to walk the territory.

This pairing is explicitly acknowledged in the Gita:

> सांख्ययोगौ पृथग्बालाः प्रवदन्ति न पण्डिताः ।
> एकमप्यास्थितः सम्यगुभयोर्विन्दते फलम् ॥
>
> *sāṃkhya-yogau pṛthag bālāḥ pravadanti na paṇḍitāḥ*
> *ekam apy āsthitaḥ samyag ubhayor vindate phalam*
>
> "Only the immature speak of Samkhya and Yoga as distinct, not the wise. One who is properly established in either obtains the fruit of both."
> — Bhagavad Gita 5.4

## Etymology: What Does "Yoga" Mean?

**योग** (yoga) — from the dhatu **युज्** (yuj, "to yoke, to unite, to join, to concentrate")

| Derivative | Meaning | Construction |
|---|---|---|
| योग (yoga) | union, discipline, method | yuj + ghañ (अ suffix) — the act or state of yoking |
| योगी / योगिन् (yogī / yogin) | practitioner of yoga | yoga + in (possessive suffix) |
| युक्त (yukta) | joined, disciplined, engaged | past participle of yuj |
| संयोग (saṃyoga) | conjunction, union | sam + yoga |
| वियोग (viyoga) | separation, disjunction | vi + yoga |
| प्रयोग (prayoga) | application, practice | pra + yoga |
| उपयोग (upayoga) | use, employment | upa + yoga |

The dhatu **युज्** has a cognate in English: "yoke" (Old English *geoc*, from Proto-Indo-European *yugom*). Also: "jugular" (Latin *jugulum*, from *jugum*, "yoke" — the neck, where the yoke sits), "conjugal" (con + jugum — yoked together), "subjugate" (sub + jugum — to bring under the yoke).

### What is being united?

This question has different answers depending on who is answering:

| Tradition | What yoga unites |
|---|---|
| **Patanjali (Yoga Sutras)** | Nothing — yoga is *nirodha* (cessation), not union. The word "yuj" here means "to concentrate, to discipline," not "to join." Yoga separates Purusha from Prakriti by stilling the mind |
| **Gita / Vedanta** | The individual self (jīvātman) with the supreme self (paramātman / Brahman) |
| **Tantra** | Shiva and Shakti — consciousness and energy |
| **Popular usage** | Body-mind union, or union of individual and cosmic |

Patanjali's usage is the most precise and the most counterintuitive: yoga is not about joining anything. It is about *disciplining the mind until consciousness recognizes itself*. The "yoking" is the yoking of the mind — bringing it under control, as an ox is yoked to a plow. You are yoking chitta, not uniting with anything.

## The Key Addition: Ishvara

Samkhya is purely dualistic: Purusha and Prakriti, nothing else. No God, no cosmic consciousness, no supreme being. Yoga accepts the entire Samkhya framework — all 25 tattvas, the gunas, the process of evolution from Prakriti — and adds one element: **ईश्वर** (Īśvara).

**ईश्वर** (īśvara) — from **ईश्** (īś, "to rule, to be master, to be capable") + **वर** (vara, suffix of eminence/capacity)

Ishvara in Yoga is not:
- A creator god — Prakriti evolves on its own through the gunas
- A personal deity who answers prayers
- A moral judge who rewards and punishes
- The sole ultimate reality (as in Vedanta's Brahman)

Ishvara in Yoga *is*:
- **पुरुषविशेष** (puruṣa-viśeṣa) — a *special* Purusha (YS 1.24)
- A Purusha who was **never** entangled with kleshas, karma, or samskaras
- An existence proof: if one Purusha was never confused, then confusion is not intrinsic to consciousness — it can be undone
- The teacher of all teachers: **स पूर्वेषामपि गुरुः कालेनानवच्छेदात्** (sa pūrveṣām api guruḥ kālenānavacchedāt — "He is the teacher even of the ancients, for he is not limited by time" — YS 1.26)
- Expressed as **OM** (प्रणव, praṇava) — the sound-form of consciousness

Ishvara serves a *functional* role in the system: he provides a focal point for meditation (ishvara pranidhana — surrender to Ishvara) and an ideal of what liberated Purusha looks like. Whether Ishvara is "real" in a theological sense is, for Patanjali's purposes, secondary. What matters is that the concept works — it gives the practitioner something to orient toward.

This is a pragmatic theology. Ishvara is useful, not dogmatic.

## Chitta — The Mind-Stuff

### The Inner Instrument

In Samkhya-Yoga psychology, the mind is not a single thing. It is an apparatus — an **अन्तःकरण** (antaḥkaraṇa, "inner instrument") — composed of three faculties:

**चित्त** (citta) — from √चित् (cit, "to perceive, to be aware") — the entire field of mental activity. Citta is the "stuff" that mind is made of — not consciousness itself (that is Purusha) but the material (prakritik) substance through which consciousness operates.

| Faculty | Sanskrit | Root | Function | Analogy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Buddhi** | बुद्धि | √बुध् (budh, "to wake, to know") | Intellect — determines, decides, discriminates. The first evolute of Prakriti (mahat). | The judge — it decides "this is a snake" or "this is a rope" |
| **Ahamkara** | अहंकार | अहम् (aham, "I") + √कृ (kṛ, "to do") | The I-maker — appropriates experience as "mine." Creates the sense of individual selfhood. | The stamp — it marks every experience with "this is happening to *me*" |
| **Manas** | मनस् | √मन् (man, "to think") | The sensory-processing mind — coordinates sense data, generates doubt (sankalpa-vikalpa). | The secretary — it gathers information from the senses and presents it for judgment |

The process of ordinary cognition:

1. **Indriyas** (senses) receive raw data from objects
2. **Manas** organizes and presents the data
3. **Ahamkara** stamps it as "my experience"
4. **Buddhi** determines what the experience means and what to do about it
5. **Purusha** — the silent witness — illuminates the entire process but does nothing

The mistake (avidya) is step 5 collapsing into step 4: Purusha identifying with buddhi's determinations, thinking "I decided this" when it actually only witnessed the decision.

### Chitta as a Lake

The traditional metaphor: chitta is like a lake. Purusha is the sun shining above it.

- When the lake is turbulent (many vrittis), the reflection of the sun is fragmented and distorted — Purusha cannot recognize itself
- When the lake is still (nirodha), the reflection is perfect — Purusha sees itself clearly
- The lake never *was* the sun. The reflection never *was* Purusha. But until the lake stills, the confusion persists

This is the entire Yoga Sutras in one image.

## The Vritti Model of Mind

### Five Types of Mental Fluctuation

Patanjali claims that every possible mental event falls into exactly five categories. This is not a rough grouping — it is an exhaustive, mutually exclusive taxonomy.

| # | Vritti | Sanskrit | Root | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Valid knowledge | **प्रमाण** (pramāṇa) | प्र + √मा (mā, "to measure") | Correct cognition through: (a) direct perception (प्रत्यक्ष, pratyakṣa), (b) inference (अनुमान, anumāna), (c) verbal testimony (आगम, āgama) | Seeing fire → "there is fire." Seeing smoke → "there is fire" (inference). A trusted teacher says "the earth orbits the sun" → accepted |
| 2 | Misconception | **विपर्यय** (viparyaya) | वि + परि + √इ (i) | False knowledge — taking something for what it is not | A rope mistaken for a snake. Seeing the self in what is not-self (the core avidya) |
| 3 | Verbal construction | **विकल्प** (vikalpa) | वि + √कॢप् (kḷp) | A mental construct based on words alone, without a corresponding reality | "The son of a barren woman." "Time flows." The concept tracks linguistically but has no referent |
| 4 | Sleep | **निद्रा** (nidrā) | नि + √द्रा (drā) | The mental activity that corresponds to the experience of absence | Deep sleep — you wake up and say "I slept well," meaning chitta recorded the experience of nothing |
| 5 | Memory | **स्मृति** (smṛti) | √स्मृ (smṛ, "to remember") | Retention and recall of past experience | Any act of remembering |

### The Radicalism of This Model

Three features make this taxonomy radical:

**First**: sleep is a vritti. The experience of unconsciousness is itself a mental activity. Even when "nothing is happening," the mind is doing something — it is registering absence. This means there is no escape from vrittis through sleep or unconsciousness. Only nirodha — the deliberate stilling — works.

**Second**: correct knowledge is also a vritti. Pramana — valid cognition, true perception — is a fluctuation of mind that must also eventually cease. This is not anti-intellectual. It is a recognition that even correct thought is still *thought* — still a wave on the lake. The goal is not to have the right thoughts. The goal is to transcend thinking entirely. Correct knowledge is a useful vritti (akliṣṭa) — it helps you get to the shore. But once you are on the shore, you leave the boat.

**Third**: each vritti can be klishta (afflicted — rooted in the kleshas, producing further suffering) or aklishta (unafflicted — not immediately causing suffering, potentially useful on the path). The same *type* of vritti can be either. Remembering a teaching that clarifies your understanding = aklishta smriti. Obsessively replaying a past humiliation = klishta smriti. Same category, different quality.

## The Eight Limbs as a Progressive System

### Outer to Inner

The eight limbs of yoga (ashtanga) form a progression from the coarsest level of experience (social behavior) to the subtlest (pure consciousness). The architecture is deliberate:

```
OUTER LIMBS (बहिरङ्ग, bahiraṅga)
├── 1. यम (Yama)        — How you relate to the WORLD
├── 2. नियम (Niyama)     — How you relate to YOURSELF
├── 3. आसन (Āsana)      — How you relate to your BODY
├── 4. प्राणायाम (Prāṇāyāma) — How you relate to your BREATH / ENERGY
└── 5. प्रत्याहार (Pratyāhāra) — How you relate to your SENSES
                                  [bridge between outer and inner]

INNER LIMBS (अन्तरङ्ग, antaraṅga)
├── 6. धारणा (Dhāraṇā)   — CONCENTRATION (mind fixed on object, with effort)
├── 7. ध्यान (Dhyāna)     — MEDITATION (continuous flow, without effort)
└── 8. समाधि (Samādhi)    — ABSORPTION (subject-object distinction dissolves)
```

### The Logic of the Progression

Each limb addresses a progressively subtler obstacle:

| Limb | Obstacle Addressed | What It Stabilizes |
|---|---|---|
| Yama | Social conflict and ethical disorder | Your relationship with other beings |
| Niyama | Inner turbulence from lack of discipline | Your personal conduct and habits |
| Asana | Physical restlessness and pain | The body — so it is no longer a distraction |
| Pranayama | Energetic imbalance and agitation | The breath and vital force — the link between body and mind |
| Pratyahara | Sensory bombardment — the mind pulled outward by sense objects | The senses — turned inward, no longer dragging attention outward |
| Dharana | Mental wandering — the mind jumps from object to object | Attention — held on one point |
| Dhyana | The effort of concentration itself — the strain of trying to focus | The flow — concentration becomes effortless |
| Samadhi | The last duality: the distinction between the one who meditates and the object of meditation | The subject-object split itself — it dissolves |

You cannot stabilize the mind (dharana) if the body is in pain (need asana) or the senses are uncontrolled (need pratyahara). You cannot control the senses if your life is in ethical chaos (need yama). The system is an engineering sequence where each stage creates the conditions necessary for the next.

### Outer vs. Inner

Patanjali explicitly distinguishes the outer three from the inner three:

The inner three (dharana, dhyana, samadhi) performed together on a single object constitute **संयम** (saṃyama) — the master technique of Pada 3. Everything before samyama is preparation. Samyama is the operation itself.

And even samyama is "outer" (bahiranga) relative to seedless samadhi (nirbija samadhi) — which transcends all objects entirely. The system keeps peeling back layers: what seemed like the goal at one level becomes the preparation for the next.

## Yoga and the Bhagavad Gita

The Gita uses the word "yoga" in multiple senses — sometimes as discipline, sometimes as method, sometimes as a specific state of being. But two chapters are directly relevant to Patanjali's system:

### Chapter 6 — ध्यानयोग (Dhyāna Yoga): The Yoga of Meditation

Chapter 6 is the Gita's most concentrated teaching on meditation practice. Krishna describes:

**The physical setup (6.11-14):**

> शुचौ देशे प्रतिष्ठाप्य स्थिरमासनमात्मनः ।
> नात्युच्छ्रितं नातिनीचं चैलाजिनकुशोत्तरम् ॥
>
> *śucau deśe pratiṣṭhāpya sthiram āsanam ātmanaḥ*
> *nātyucchritaṃ nātinīcaṃ cailājinakuśottaram*
>
> "In a clean place, establishing a firm seat for oneself — not too high, not too low — with cloth, deerskin, and kusha grass layered..."
> — BG 6.11

This parallels Patanjali's **स्थिरसुखमासनम्** (sthira-sukham āsanam) — a steady, comfortable seat. The Gita and the Sutras agree: the body must be settled before the mind can be.

**The mind in meditation (6.19):**

> यथा दीपो निवातस्थो नेङ्गते सोपमा स्मृता ।
> योगिनो यतचित्तस्य युञ्जतो योगमात्मनः ॥
>
> *yathā dīpo nivātastho neṅgate sopamā smṛtā*
> *yogino yata-cittasya yuñjato yogam ātmanaḥ*
>
> "As a lamp in a windless place does not flicker — such is the simile for the yogi of controlled mind, practicing the yoga of the self."
> — BG 6.19

The unwavering lamp = the mind in dhyana (steady, effortless flow). Patanjali's **प्रत्ययैकतानता** (pratyaya-ekatānatā, "single-stretchedness of cognition") is exactly this image: a flame that does not waver.

**Arjuna's objection (6.33-34):**

> चञ्चलं हि मनः कृष्ण प्रमाथि बलवद्दृढम् ।
> तस्याहं निग्रहं मन्ये वायोरिव सुदुष्करम् ॥
>
> *cañcalaṃ hi manaḥ kṛṣṇa pramāthi balavad dṛḍham*
> *tasyāhaṃ nigrahaṃ manye vāyor iva suduṣkaram*
>
> "The mind is restless, turbulent, powerful, obstinate, O Krishna. I consider restraining it as difficult as restraining the wind."
> — BG 6.34

Krishna's response (6.35): **अभ्यासेन तु कौन्तेय वैराग्येण च गृह्यते** — "By abhyasa and vairagya it is restrained, O son of Kunti." This is a direct echo (or source) of YS 1.12: **अभ्यासवैराग्याभ्यां तन्निरोधः**. The two texts share the same prescription.

### Chapter 2 — स्थितप्रज्ञ (Sthitaprajña): The One of Steady Wisdom

In BG 2.54, Arjuna asks: "What is the description of one whose wisdom is steady (sthitaprajna)? How does such a person sit, speak, walk?"

Krishna's answer (2.55-72) is a portrait of the person who has achieved what Patanjali calls nirodha. Key verses:

> **यदा संहरते चायं कूर्मोऽङ्गानीव सर्वशः ।**
> **इन्द्रियाणीन्द्रियार्थेभ्यस्तस्य प्रज्ञा प्रतिष्ठिता ॥**
>
> *yadā saṃharate cāyaṃ kūrmo'ṅgānīva sarvaśaḥ*
> *indriyāṇīndriyārthebhyas tasya prajñā pratiṣṭhitā*
>
> "When one withdraws the senses from sense-objects on all sides, as a tortoise draws its limbs [into its shell], that person's wisdom is steady."
> — BG 2.58

This is **प्रत्याहार** (pratyāhāra) — the fifth limb — described in a Gita context centuries before (or contemporaneous with) Patanjali's systematization.

> **रागद्वेषवियुक्तैस्तु विषयानिन्द्रियैश्चरन् ।**
> **आत्मवश्यैर्विधेयात्मा प्रसादमधिगच्छति ॥**
>
> *rāga-dveṣa-viyuktais tu viṣayān indriyaiś caran*
> *ātma-vaśyair vidheyātmā prasādam adhigacchati*
>
> "But one who moves among sense-objects with senses free from attachment and aversion, self-controlled — that person attains clarity (prasada)."
> — BG 2.64

Free from **राग** (rāga) and **द्वेष** (dveṣa) — two of Patanjali's five kleshas. The Gita's sthitaprajna is Patanjali's yogi who has overcome the afflictions. Same person, described from different angles.

## Society of Thoughts: Yoga as a Computational Framework

### Chitta Vritti Nirodha as Noise Reduction

Consider a neural network. Raw sensory data enters. The network processes it through layers of weights and activations. At each layer, noise accumulates — irrelevant features, spurious correlations, artifacts of the training process. The output is a mixture of signal and noise.

Chitta is the network. Vrittis are the activations — the patterns of activity propagating through the layers. Some activations correspond to genuine features of reality (pramana — valid knowledge). Some are hallucinations generated by the network's own architecture (viparyaya — misconception). Some are artifacts of the symbolic layer with no grounding in input data (vikalpa — verbal construction). Some are the network's background noise in idle state (nidra — sleep). Some are cached outputs from previous inputs (smriti — memory).

**Nirodha is not unplugging the network. It is achieving a zero-noise state where only the ground truth signal remains.**

In signal processing: the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) approaches infinity not by amplifying the signal but by eliminating the noise. That is exactly Patanjali's approach. You do not seek louder truth. You eliminate every source of distortion until truth is all that remains.

The five kleshas are the systematic noise sources:
- **Avidya** = systematic bias in the model's architecture — the network is structured to confuse features with the observer
- **Asmita** = the model reifying itself as an entity rather than recognizing itself as a process
- **Raga** = positive reinforcement loops that amplify certain patterns regardless of their truth value
- **Dvesha** = negative reinforcement loops that suppress patterns regardless of their truth value
- **Abhinivesha** = the model's resistance to being retrained or shut down — the system's self-preservation bias

### The Eight Limbs as a Training Pipeline

If the kleshas are the noise sources, the eight limbs are a systematic denoising pipeline. Each limb addresses a different layer of the system:

```

    │
    ▼
```

### Where the Analogy Breaks — And Why That Matters

Every analogy has a failure point. Here are the ones that matter:

**1. Purusha has no analogue in computation.** A neural network has no witness. It processes; it does not experience. The "hard problem of consciousness" is precisely the gap between processing (what networks do) and experiencing (what Purusha does). Yoga's claim is that this gap is not a puzzle to be solved but a fact to be recognized — consciousness is not *produced by* the processing; it *illuminates* it.

**2. Nirodha is not shutdown.** Turning off a computer is not nirodha. Nirodha is the cessation of activity *while awareness continues*. There is no computational analogue for this because computation requires activity — a halted computer is not "aware" of its halt. Purusha in nirodha is.

**3. The model is not trying to get better.** In ML, the objective is to minimize a loss function — to make the model's outputs match some external ground truth. In yoga, there is no external ground truth. Purusha *is* the ground truth, and it was never wrong. The "training" is chitta (Prakriti's instrument) learning to stop obstructing Purusha's self-recognition. It is less like training a model and more like cleaning a window — the landscape was always there.

These failures are not weaknesses of the analogy. They mark the exact boundary where Samkhya-Yoga's metaphysics makes claims that no materialist framework can replicate. The computational metaphor is useful for understanding the *method* (the eight limbs as a systematic process). It is useless for understanding the *ontology* (what Purusha is and why it matters). That is the boundary between engineering and philosophy.

## Summary: Yoga's Position in the Landscape

| Question | Yoga's Answer |
|---|---|
| What is real? | Purusha (consciousness) and Prakriti (matter) — Samkhya's dualism, accepted |
| What is the problem? | Purusha misidentifies with Prakriti's modifications (chitta vrittis) |
| What is liberation? | Kaivalya — Purusha's recognition of itself as separate from all Prakritic activity |
| How do you get there? | Systematic discipline: citta-vritti-nirodha, achieved through the eight limbs |
| What about God? | Ishvara is a special Purusha — useful for practice, not metaphysically necessary |
| Relationship to Samkhya? | Samkhya = the diagnosis. Yoga = the treatment |
| Relationship to Vedanta? | Vedanta will later argue that Purusha and Prakriti are themselves not ultimately real — that only Brahman is. Yoga does not go that far. It stops at the Samkhya dualism and provides a practice to live within it |
| Relationship to the Gita? | The Gita's Chapter 6 (Dhyana Yoga) and Chapter 2 (Sthitaprajna) describe the yogic state. But the Gita synthesizes yoga with karma (action), bhakti (devotion), and jnana (knowledge) — a broader integration than Patanjali's purely meditative system |
