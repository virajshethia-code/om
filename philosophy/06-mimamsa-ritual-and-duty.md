# Mimamsa — Ritual and Duty

## Why Mimamsa Matters

Mimamsa is the most underappreciated school in the Shaddarshana. It gets overlooked because people rush past it to reach Vedanta — the glamorous sibling that talks about Brahman and cosmic consciousness. But this is like skipping the compiler to admire the program. Mimamsa is the school that asks: what do the Vedas actually *mean*? How do you read them? What counts as a valid interpretation? And what are the Vedas fundamentally *for*?

Its answer is startling: the Vedas are not about God, not about the nature of reality, not about metaphysical truths. The Vedas are about **what you should do**. Their primary content is *injunction* — commands for action. Everything else (stories, descriptions, praises) exists only to support those commands.

Mimamsa built the hermeneutics — the science of interpretation — that every other school of Indian philosophy, law, and theology depends on. The rules Mimamsa developed for reading Vedic texts became the rules for reading *all* authoritative texts in India. If Nyaya gave India its logic, Mimamsa gave India its jurisprudence.

---

## Etymology: What Does "Mimamsa" Mean?

**मीमांसा** (mīmāṃsā) — from the dhatu **मन्** (man, "to think, to reflect, to investigate")

The form मीमांसा is a **desiderative** (सन्नन्त, sannanta) construction — it expresses the *desire* to do the root action. So:

- **मन्** (man) = to think
- **मीमांसा** (mīmāṃsā) = the desire to think deeply → "profound reflection," "sustained investigation," "critical inquiry"

The desiderative is key. This is not casual thinking. It is the *drive* to investigate — systematic, relentless, exhaustive analysis. Mimamsa investigates the Vedas the way a compiler parses source code: every token matters, every structure has a reason, nothing is ornamental.

| Sanskrit | Meaning | Construction |
|---|---|---|
| मन् (man) | to think, to reflect | root dhatu |
| मनस् (manas) | mind | man + as (suffix) |
| मानस (mānasa) | mental, of the mind | from manas |
| मीमांसा (mīmāṃsā) | deep inquiry, investigation | desiderative of man |
| मीमांसक (mīmāṃsaka) | one who investigates | mīmāṃsā + ka (agent) |

## The Two Mimamsas

The name "Mimamsa" actually covers two schools — two investigations of the same Vedic corpus, aimed at different layers:

| | **पूर्वमीमांसा** (Pūrva Mīmāṃsā) | **उत्तरमीमांसा** (Uttara Mīmāṃsā) |
|---|---|---|
| **Meaning** | Prior/Earlier Investigation | Later/Higher Investigation |
| **Also called** | Karma Mimamsa, or simply "Mimamsa" | Vedanta ("end of the Vedas") |
| **Focus** | The कर्मकाण्ड (karmakāṇḍa) — ritual action portion | The ज्ञानकाण्ड (jñānakāṇḍa) — knowledge portion |
| **Vedic layers** | Samhita + Brahmana | Aranyaka + Upanishad |
| **Central question** | What must we DO? (dharma) | What must we KNOW? (brahman) |
| **Founder** | जैमिनि (Jaimini) | बादरायण (Bādarāyaṇa) / Vyasa |
| **Root text** | Mimamsa Sutras (~200 BCE) | Brahma Sutras (~200 BCE) |
| **Core concern** | Correct interpretation of Vedic injunctions | Nature of ultimate reality |

### Breaking down the names

- **पूर्व** (pūrva) = prior, earlier, first — from **पृ** (pṛ, "to fill, to go before"). Refers both to textual sequence (the earlier portions of the Vedas) and logical priority (you must understand action before understanding being)
- **उत्तर** (uttara) = later, higher, beyond — from **उद्** (ud, "upward") + **तर** (tara, comparative suffix). The later/higher investigation

This lesson covers Purva Mimamsa only. Uttara Mimamsa (Vedanta) gets its own dedicated treatment — it is vast enough to require it. But understand: these two were originally conceived as *one* investigation in two parts. Jaimini and Badarayana cross-reference each other. Purva Mimamsa establishes what the Vedas command; Uttara Mimamsa establishes what the Vedas reveal.

## Jaimini and the Mimamsa Sutras

### जैमिनि (Jaimini) — The Founder

Jaimini is attributed as the author of the **मीमांसासूत्र** (Mīmāṃsā Sūtra), also called the **जैमिनीयसूत्र** (Jaiminīya Sūtra). He is mentioned in the Mahabharata as a disciple of Vyasa, the great compiler. Historically, Jaimini is a semi-legendary figure, like Kapila for Samkhya — the name represents a tradition as much as an individual.

### The Text: Mimamsa Sutras (~200 BCE)

The Mimamsa Sutras are the **longest sutra text in all of Indian philosophy**: approximately 2,745 sutras organized into 12 chapters (अध्याय, adhyāya), each divided into quarters (पाद, pāda).

To appreciate the scale:

| Text | Approximate Sutra Count |
|---|---|
| Brahma Sutras (Vedanta) | ~555 |
| Yoga Sutras (Patanjali) | 196 |
| Nyaya Sutras (Gautama) | ~528 |
| Vaisheshika Sutras (Kanada) | ~370 |
| **Mimamsa Sutras (Jaimini)** | **~2,745** |

This is not accidental. Mimamsa's project is the most technically demanding: to provide rules for interpreting *every type* of Vedic statement, resolving *every type* of interpretive conflict, and determining *every type* of ritual obligation. It is a complete hermeneutical system. The length reflects the scope.

### The Opening Sutra

The very first sutra announces the entire project:

> अथातो धर्मजिज्ञासा
> *athāto dharmajijñāsā*
> "Now, therefore, the inquiry into dharma."

Breaking it down:

- **अथ** (atha) = now — a formal beginning marker, indicating prerequisites have been met (the student has studied the Vedas)
- **अतः** (ataḥ) = therefore — because the Vedas have been studied, the investigation into their meaning can now begin
- **धर्म** (dharma) = duty, that which sustains — from **धृ** (dhṛ, to hold, to sustain)
- **जिज्ञासा** (jijñāsā) = the desire to know — desiderative of **ज्ञा** (jñā, to know)

Compare this with the opening of the Brahma Sutras:

> अथातो ब्रह्मजिज्ञासा
> *athāto brahmajijñāsā*
> "Now, therefore, the inquiry into Brahman."

The parallelism is deliberate. Same structure, different object. Mimamsa investigates dharma (what to do). Vedanta investigates Brahman (what is real). Together, they cover the full scope of Vedic inquiry: action and knowledge, करणीय (karaṇīya, "to be done") and ज्ञेय (jñeya, "to be known").

### Key Commentators

The Mimamsa tradition splits into two major sub-schools based on their commentators:

| | **Kumarila Bhatta** | **Prabhakara Mishra** |
|---|---|---|
| **Period** | ~7th century CE | ~7th century CE |
| **Major work** | श्लोकवार्तिक (Ślokavārttika) | बृहती (Bṛhatī) |
| **Sub-school name** | भाट्ट (Bhāṭṭa) | प्राभाकर (Prābhākara) |
| **Theory of meaning** | अभिहितान्वय (Abhihitānvaya) | अन्विताभिधान (Anvitābhidhāna) |
| **Number of pramanas** | 6 | 5 |
| **On self-awareness** | Knowledge is known by inference | Knowledge is self-luminous |

Both are rigorous. Both are orthodox. Their disagreements are technical and deep — more on their theories of meaning below.

## Core Thesis: The Vedas Are Injunctions

This is the central claim of Mimamsa, and it is radical:

**The Vedas are eternal, authorless, and their primary purpose is to issue commands — vidhis — telling you what to do.**

Let us unpack each piece.

### 1. The Vedas Are Eternal (नित्य, nitya)

Mimamsa holds that the Vedas have always existed. They were not composed at any point in time, by anyone — human or divine. They are **अपौरुषेय** (apauruṣeya, "not of human origin"):

- **अ** (a) = not
- **पौरुषेय** (pauruṣeya) = derived from a person — from **पुरुष** (puruṣa, person)

This is stronger than saying "God wrote them." Mimamsa says *nobody* wrote them — not even God. The Vedas are part of the fabric of reality itself, like the laws of mathematics. They are discovered, not authored. The rishis who "heard" (श्रुति) them were receivers, not creators.

Why does Mimamsa insist on this? Because authority depends on it. A text authored by a person is subject to that person's limitations — their ignorance, their bias, their errors. An authorless text has no such vulnerability. The Vedas are authoritative precisely because no author's fallibility can contaminate them.

### 2. The Vedas Are Primarily Injunctive

Mimamsa classifies Vedic statements into categories:

| Type | Sanskrit | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Injunction** | विधि (vidhi) | Commands an action | "One who desires heaven should perform the Agnihotra" |
| **Prohibition** | निषेध (niṣedha) | Forbids an action | "One should not eat kalañja (a forbidden plant)" |
| **Mantra** | मन्त्र (mantra) | Accompanies ritual action | The hymns recited during ritual |
| **Name** | नामधेय (nāmadheya) | Names a ritual or entity | "This sacrifice is called Darshapurnamasa" |
| **Explanatory passage** | अर्थवाद (arthavāda) | Praises or explains to motivate | "The wind is indeed the swiftest deity" |
| **Restrictive rule** | नियम (niyama) | Restricts options within an injunction | "One should pound the rice (not grind it)" |

The hierarchy is clear: **vidhi is supreme**. Everything else — mantras, arthavadas, names — exists to serve the injunction. If a Vedic passage does not directly or indirectly support an injunction, it has no independent purpose according to Mimamsa.

This means the famous Vedic descriptions of gods, cosmological narratives, philosophical reflections — all of these are classified as **अर्थवाद** (arthavāda), supplementary passages whose purpose is to motivate compliance with injunctions. The story of Indra slaying Vritra is not told for its own sake; it is told to inspire confidence in the rituals addressed to Indra.

### विधि (Vidhi) — Injunction

**विधि** (vidhi) — from **वि** (vi, intensifier/separator) + **धा** (dhā, "to place, to establish")

vidhi = "that which establishes, that which directs" — a prescriptive statement, a command

Mimamsa recognizes four types of vidhi:

| Type | Sanskrit | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Originating injunction** | उत्पत्तिविधि (utpattividhi) | Establishes a new ritual | "One who desires heaven should sacrifice" |
| **Qualifying injunction** | गुणविधि (guṇavidhi) | Specifies a subsidiary detail | "He should sacrifice with curds" |
| **Exclusive injunction** | नियमविधि (niyamavidhi) | Restricts among alternatives | "He should sacrifice with rice (not barley)" |
| **Prohibitive injunction** | प्रतिषेधविधि (pratiṣedhavidhi) | Forbids a specific action | "He should not look at the sun during the ritual" |

The test for whether a Vedic sentence is a genuine vidhi is the presence of the **optative mood** (लिङ् लकार, liṅ lakāra) — the verb form that expresses "should" or "ought." When you see a verb in the optative in a Vedic passage, you are looking at an injunction. This is a grammatical criterion, not a theological one. The structure of the language itself marks what is command and what is description.

★ **Insight**: The optative mood as a formal marker of injunction is remarkably close to how modern programming languages distinguish statements from expressions, or how deontic logic distinguishes "ought" from "is." Mimamsa built a formal system for detecting normative content in natural language — 2,000 years before deontic logic was formalized by von Wright in 1951.

## Shabda-Brahman: The Eternal Word

One of Mimamsa's most philosophically bold claims is the **नित्यत्व** (nityatva, eternality) of the relationship between word and meaning.

### शब्दब्रह्मन् (Śabdabrahman) — Language as Ultimate Reality

- **शब्द** (śabda) = word, sound — from **शब्द्** (śabd, to sound)
- **ब्रह्मन्** (brahman) = the ultimate, the expansive — from **बृह्** (bṛh, to grow, to expand)

For Mimamsa, Vedic language is not a human invention. The connection between a word and its meaning is **intrinsic and eternal** (स्वाभाविक, svābhāvika), not conventional (सांकेतिक, sāṅketika).

This directly opposes the position of Ferdinand de Saussure (1916), who argued that the relationship between signifier and signified is arbitrary — a matter of social convention. It also opposes the position of the Buddhist logician Dignaga, who argued the same from within the Indian tradition. Mimamsa says: no. The word "cow" (गो, go) does not *happen* to mean "cow" because speakers agreed on it. The word *is* connected to the thing by the nature of reality itself. The word is eternal; the thing is eternal; the relation is eternal.

### How does Mimamsa defend this?

The argument proceeds through several steps:

1. **We never witness the creation of a word-meaning relationship.** Children learn words from adults, who learned from their elders, in an infinite regress. Nobody ever *created* the connection between "go" and cow. If the connection were conventional, there must have been a first convention — but no such event is witnessed or remembered.

2. **The Vedas presuppose these relationships.** If word-meaning relations were created at some point, they would be subject to the creator's knowledge and intent, making the Vedas dependent on a fallible author. This contradicts apaurusheyatva.

3. **Universals are real and eternal.** The word "cow" refers not to a particular cow but to the **universal** (जाति, jāti) "cow-ness" (गोत्व, gotva). Universals do not come into being or perish. Therefore the word that denotes the universal is also eternal.

This is a full-fledged philosophy of language — a realist semantics — developed with extraordinary rigor. It may be wrong (most modern linguists would say it is), but it is not naive. It is a systematic position defended against sophisticated objections over centuries.

★ **Insight**: Mimamsa's position is not as alien to modern thought as it first appears. Consider mathematical Platonism: the number 2 does not exist because someone invented it. It *is*, and our symbol for it is a discovery, not an invention. Mimamsa extends this Platonic realism to *all* of Vedic language. Whether you accept this or not, the structure of the argument — that some symbol-referent relationships are necessary rather than conventional — has active defenders in contemporary philosophy of mathematics and formal semantics.

## Theories of Meaning: How Sentences Work

Mimamsa's two sub-schools disagree on a fundamental question in the philosophy of language: **How do individual word-meanings combine to form sentence-meaning?**

### अन्विताभिधान (Anvitābhidhāna) — Prabhakara's Theory

- **अन्वित** (anvita) = connected, related — from **अनु** (anu, along) + **इत** (ita, gone, from इ/i, to go)
- **अभिधान** (abhidhāna) = denotation, naming — from **अभि** (abhi, toward) + **धा** (dhā, to place)
- anvitābhidhāna = "denotation of the connected" → words *directly* denote their meanings as already connected in the sentence

**Prabhakara's claim**: Words do not first denote isolated meanings that must then be assembled. A word's meaning is *always already* relational — you never encounter the meaning of a word in isolation. When you hear "bring the cow," you do not first grasp "bring" and "cow" separately and then combine them. You grasp "bringing-of-cow" as a unified meaning. The sentence is the primary unit of meaning; words are abstractions from sentences.

### अभिहितान्वय (Abhihitānvaya) — Kumarila's Theory

- **अभिहित** (abhihita) = denoted, expressed — from **अभि** (abhi, toward) + **हित** (hita, placed, from धा/dhā)
- **अन्वय** (anvaya) = connection, syntactic relation — from **अनु** (anu, along) + **अय** (aya, going)
- abhihitānvaya = "connection of the denoted" → words first denote individual meanings, which are then connected by a separate operation

**Kumarila's claim**: Words *do* first denote individual meanings. "Bring" means bringing. "Cow" means cow. Sentence-meaning is then constructed by a secondary operation that connects these individual meanings based on three criteria:

1. **आकाङ्क्षा** (ākāṅkṣā) — mutual expectancy: words syntactically require each other ("bring" expects an object)
2. **योग्यता** (yogyatā) — semantic compatibility: the meanings must be logically compatible ("water the plants" is compatible; "water the fire" is not — in normal contexts)
3. **सन्निधि** (sannidhi) — contiguity: the words must be spoken in proximity (you cannot combine a word from today's sentence with one from yesterday's)

### Why this matters

This is not a trivial scholastic distinction. It is one of the deepest questions in the philosophy of language:

- **Is meaning fundamentally compositional?** (Kumarila: yes — build up from parts)
- **Or is meaning fundamentally holistic?** (Prabhakara: yes — the whole precedes the parts)

This debate anticipated the compositionality principle in modern formal semantics (Frege, Montague) versus holistic meaning theories (the later Wittgenstein, Quine's meaning holism). Kumarila is a proto-Fregean. Prabhakara is a proto-Wittgensteinian. The debate is unresolved in both traditions.

★ **Insight**: In machine learning terms, Kumarila's model is a **pipeline**: tokenize → embed individual tokens → compose via attention/syntax. Prabhakara's model is closer to **end-to-end learning**: the sentence embedding is not built from word embeddings but learned directly. Modern NLP has found that both approaches have merit — which is roughly where this 1,300-year-old debate also stands.

## Dharma: The Central Concept

### धर्म (Dharma) — "That Which Sustains"

**धर्म** (dharma) — from the dhatu **धृ** (dhṛ, "to hold, to bear, to sustain")

- **धृ** (dhṛ) + **म** (ma, suffix indicating "that which") = dharma = "that which holds/sustains"

Dharma is the most untranslatable word in Sanskrit. It is *not* "religion." It is *not* "morality" in the Kantian sense. It is not "law" in the legal sense, though it includes all of these.

Jaimini defines dharma in his second sutra:

> चोदनालक्षणोऽर्थो धर्मः
> *codanālakṣaṇo'rtho dharmaḥ*
> "Dharma is that purpose/good which is characterized by Vedic injunction (codanā)."

Breaking it down:

- **चोदना** (codanā) = Vedic injunction, the impelling command — from **चुद्** (cud, "to impel, to urge")
- **लक्षण** (lakṣaṇa) = characterized by, defined by — from **लक्ष्** (lakṣ, "to mark, to indicate")
- **अर्थ** (artha) = purpose, meaning, goal — from **अर्थ्** (arth, "to strive for")

So: dharma is **that purposeful good which is known through Vedic command**. You cannot know dharma through perception (you cannot see duty with your eyes), through inference (you cannot logically deduce it from natural facts), or through any other means. Dharma is known only through **शब्द** (śabda) — authoritative verbal testimony, specifically Vedic injunction.

This is a radical epistemological claim. Dharma is **supersensible** (अतीन्द्रिय, atīndriya) — beyond the reach of the senses and inference. The only valid source of knowledge about what you *ought* to do is the Veda. This does not mean you need the Vedas for everyday decisions. It means that for questions of ultimate duty — the actions that produce unseen karmic results — only Vedic authority can guide you.

### Dharma vs. other definitions

| School | Dharma means... |
|---|---|
| **Mimamsa** | That which is enjoined by the Vedas — duty as ritually defined |
| **Vedanta** | The cosmic order and one's role in it — aligned with Brahman |
| **Buddhism** | The teachings of the Buddha; also, the constituents of experience (dharmas, lowercase) |
| **Jainism** | The principle of motion; also, right conduct |
| **Common usage** | Religion, righteousness, duty, law, nature, truth |

Mimamsa's definition is the most precise and the most restrictive. Dharma is not a vague cosmic goodness. It is a specific set of obligations defined by specific texts, knowable only through those texts.

## Apurva: The Unseen Karmic Mechanism

Here is Mimamsa's most inventive metaphysical concept:

### अपूर्व (Apūrva) — The Unprecedented Force

- **अ** (a) = not
- **पूर्व** (pūrva) = prior, previously existing
- apūrva = "that which did not exist before" — something unprecedented, a new force brought into being

**The problem**: A ritual is performed today. Its promised result (say, heaven after death) comes much later. What connects the present action to the future result? The action is completed and finished — the fire has burned out, the offerings have been consumed. What bridges the temporal gap?

**Mimamsa's answer**: The correctly performed ritual generates an **apurva** — an unseen, supersensible potency that inheres in the performer's soul and produces the promised result at the appropriate future time.

Apurva is:
- **Not perceptible** — you cannot see or measure it
- **Not inferrable** — you cannot deduce it from observable premises alone
- **Known only through scripture** — the Vedas tell you that certain actions produce certain results; the mechanism is apurva
- **Generated by correct ritual performance** — imprecise or incorrect ritual produces no apurva (or defective apurva)
- **Stored in the agent** — it stays with the performer until it produces its effect

### Apurva and Karma

Apurva is Mimamsa's version of the karmic mechanism. Other schools (Samkhya, Yoga, Vedanta) have their own accounts of how actions produce future results. Mimamsa's is the most mechanistic: a well-defined ritual produces a well-defined force that produces a well-defined outcome. It is karmic engineering.

> अपूर्वं हि कर्मणः फलम्
> *apūrvaṃ hi karmaṇaḥ phalam*
> "The fruit of action is indeed apurva."

This means that when the Vedas say "one who desires heaven should perform the Jyotishtoma sacrifice," the causal chain is:

```
Correct performance of Jyotishtoma
        ↓
  Apurva is generated
        ↓
  Apurva inheres in the performer
        ↓
  At the appropriate time, apurva produces its effect
        ↓
  The performer attains heaven
```

★ **Insight**: Apurva functions like a **deferred callback** in programming. The ritual is the function call. Apurva is the promise object — it encapsulates the future result of a completed action. The result is not immediate; it is stored and executed later. The ritual's "return value" is asynchronous. This is a remarkably clean model of action-at-a-temporal-distance, and it solves the same problem that karma theory always faces: how do present actions produce future consequences when the present action no longer exists?

## The Six Marks of Interpretation (षड्लिङ्ग, Ṣaḍliṅga)

Mimamsa's most practically influential contribution is its system of hermeneutics — rules for determining the meaning of a text when it is ambiguous or when multiple interpretations are possible.

The **षड्लिङ्ग** (ṣaḍliṅga, "six marks/indicators") are:

- **षट्** (ṣaṭ) = six
- **लिङ्ग** (liṅga) = mark, sign, indicator — from **लिङ्ग्** (liṅg, "to mark, to characterize")

| # | Sanskrit | Transliteration | Meaning | How It Works |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | उपक्रम-उपसंहार | upakrama-upasaṃhāra | Beginning and conclusion | The main topic is what is stated at both the opening and closing of a passage |
| 2 | अभ्यास | abhyāsa | Repetition | What is repeated is important — repetition signals emphasis |
| 3 | अपूर्वता | apūrvatā | Novelty | What is stated for the first time (not available from any other source) is the unique teaching of the passage |
| 4 | फल | phala | Result/fruit | The stated result reveals the purpose of the teaching |
| 5 | अर्थवाद | arthavāda | Explanatory praise | Eulogy or praise passages indicate what the text values and recommends |
| 6 | उपपत्ति | upapatti | Logical reasoning | Rational arguments within the text support the main thesis |

### How to apply them

When you encounter a Vedic passage (or any authoritative text) and need to determine its primary teaching, you check all six marks. The more marks that point to a particular interpretation, the stronger that interpretation is. If all six converge, the meaning is certain.

**Example**: Suppose a passage begins by discussing the Agnihotra ritual (upakrama), repeatedly emphasizes fire offerings (abhyasa), introduces a specific technique not found elsewhere (apurvata), promises heaven as the result (phala), praises fire as the greatest deity (arthavada), and reasons that fire connects the human and divine realms (upapatti). All six marks converge on the Agnihotra as the primary teaching. The interpretation is settled.

### The influence of Shadlinga

These six rules became the standard hermeneutical toolkit for all of Indian intellectual life:

- **Vedanta** adopted them wholesale. Shankara uses the shadlinga extensively in his commentaries to determine the teaching of each Upanishad.
- **Dharmashastra** (legal tradition) uses them to interpret smriti texts and resolve conflicts between legal sources.
- **Nyaya** (logic) incorporated textual interpretation principles derived from Mimamsa.
- **Indian judicial tradition** to this day uses principles traceable to Mimamsa hermeneutics for statutory interpretation.

★ **Insight**: The shadlinga is a formal method for resolving ambiguity in authoritative texts. It is functionally equivalent to principles of statutory interpretation in modern jurisprudence: *ejusdem generis* (interpret by context), the mischief rule (look at the purpose), the rule against surplusage (every word matters). Mimamsa developed a complete theory of legal interpretation centuries before Roman jurists tackled the same problems.

## Mimamsa's Surprising Atheism

This is the part that shocks most people: **Purva Mimamsa, in its classical form, has no need for God.**

Jaimini's Mimamsa Sutras do not posit an Ishvara (God, controller, cosmic lord). The system is complete without one. Here is why:

### Why God is unnecessary

1. **The Vedas are authorless.** They do not need a divine author. They are eternal and self-existent. Introducing God as their author would actually *weaken* their authority (because a personal author is fallible).

2. **The karmic mechanism (apurva) is automatic.** Ritual produces apurva; apurva produces results. This is a law-like mechanism, not a personal dispensation. No divine agent is needed to distribute rewards and punishments.

3. **Dharma is defined by injunction, not by divine will.** An action is dharmic because the Vedas enjoin it, not because God commands it. This avoids the Euthyphro dilemma entirely: duty is not grounded in divine will, so the question "Is it good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?" does not arise.

4. **The universe is eternal.** Mimamsa accepts no creation event and therefore no creator. The world, like the Vedas, always was. Time is cyclical. There is no first moment requiring a first cause.

### Kumarila's explicit arguments against God

Kumarila Bhatta, in his Shlokavartika, provides direct arguments against the existence of Ishvara:

- **The problem of evil**: If God is omniscient and omnipotent and benevolent, suffering should not exist. But it does.
- **The problem of motivation**: An omniscient, self-sufficient being would have no unfulfilled desire and therefore no motive to create. Creation requires motivation; perfection precludes it.
- **Parsimony**: The Vedas, dharma, apurva, and the eternal cycle of the world explain everything we need to explain. God is an unnecessary hypothesis.

This makes Purva Mimamsa one of the **explicitly atheistic orthodox schools** — atheistic not in the sense of rejecting the Vedas (it upholds them absolutely) but in the sense of denying a personal creator-God. The Vedas are more authoritative than any God could be, because they are *not* the product of a mind.

### Later theistic modifications

Later Mimamsakas — especially those influenced by Vedanta — softened this stance. The integration of Purva and Uttara Mimamsa in the medieval period brought God back into the picture. But the classical Mimamsa position is clear: the system works without Ishvara, and introducing Ishvara introduces more problems than it solves.

## Mimamsa's Theory of Knowledge (Pramanas)

Mimamsa is unusually generous in its epistemology, accepting more sources of valid knowledge than most other schools:

### Kumarila (Bhatta school): Six Pramanas

| # | Pramana | Sanskrit | Meaning | What It Validates |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Perception | प्रत्यक्ष (pratyakṣa) | Direct sensory contact | "I see fire" |
| 2 | Inference | अनुमान (anumāna) | Reasoning from observed sign to unobserved fact | "Where there is smoke, there is fire" |
| 3 | Verbal testimony | शब्द (śabda) | Authoritative statement (especially Vedic) | "The Veda says perform Agnihotra" |
| 4 | Comparison | उपमान (upamāna) | Knowing by similarity | "A gavaya is like a cow" |
| 5 | Postulation | अर्थापत्ति (arthāpatti) | Necessary assumption to resolve a contradiction | "Devadatta is fat but doesn't eat by day → he must eat at night" |
| 6 | Non-apprehension | अनुपलब्धि (anupalabdhi) | Knowledge of absence through non-perception | "There is no pot on the table" (known by not seeing a pot) |

### Prabhakara: Five Pramanas

Prabhakara accepts the first five but rejects anupalabdhi (non-apprehension), arguing that absence is known through perception itself, not through a separate pramana.

### The key pramana: Shabda

For Mimamsa, **shabda** (verbal testimony) is the most important pramana — and specifically, Vedic shabda. This is because dharma, the central object of Mimamsa's inquiry, is **atindriya** (beyond the senses). You cannot see dharma, you cannot infer it, you cannot compare it to anything. You can only know it through the authoritative word of the Vedas.

Mimamsa also holds **स्वतःप्रामाण्य** (svataḥprāmāṇya) — the **intrinsic validity** of all cognition. Every cognition is presumed valid until proven otherwise. You do not need to justify a belief before accepting it; you only need to explain it if it turns out to be wrong. This is the epistemological equivalent of "innocent until proven guilty."

## The Structure of Vedic Ritual: Mimamsa's Analysis

Mimamsa provides an elaborate taxonomy of ritual elements. Understanding this structure reveals the school's extraordinary analytical precision.

### Principal and Subsidiary

Every Vedic ritual (यज्ञ, yajña) has:

| Element | Sanskrit | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Principal rite** | प्रधान (pradhāna) | The main sacrificial act that directly produces the result |
| **Subsidiary elements** | अङ्ग (aṅga) | Components that support the principal — mantras, materials, procedures |

The relationship between pradhana and anga is hierarchical: subsidiaries exist *for the sake of* the principal. A mantra recited during a ritual has no independent purpose — it serves the ritual. A specific offering material is specified because it serves the ritual's goal.

### Types of Sacrifice

| Type | Sanskrit | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Desire-motivated** | काम्य (kāmya) | Performed to obtain a specific result (heaven, wealth, progeny) |
| **Obligatory** | नित्य (nitya) | Must be performed daily or regularly — failure incurs sin |
| **Occasional** | नैमित्तिक (naimitikka) | Performed on specific occasions (birth, death, eclipses) |
| **Expiatory** | प्रायश्चित्त (prāyaścitta) | Performed to remedy errors or sins |

The obligatory rituals (nitya) are philosophically the most interesting. They produce no positive result — you do not *gain* anything by performing them. But *failing* to perform them produces **प्रत्यवाय** (pratyavāya, "negative consequence, sin"). This means dharma includes not only "do this to gain that" but "do this because not doing it is wrong." Duty is not always instrumental. Some duties are categorical.

## Society of Thoughts: Language as Executable Code

Here is where Mimamsa's framework connects to the deepest themes of this project.

### Vidhi as Function Call

Consider how Mimamsa treats a Vedic injunction:

> स्वर्गकामो यजेत
> *svargakāmo yajeta*
> "One who desires heaven should sacrifice."

This is not a description of reality. It is not a statement of fact. It does not say "people who sacrifice go to heaven" (that would be an arthavada — an explanatory passage). It says: **if you desire heaven, sacrifice.** It is a conditional command.

In programming terms:

```
function yajeta(agent) {
    // precondition: agent.desires("svarga")
    // action: perform sacrifice according to specified procedure
    // effect: generates apurva → deferred result: svarga
    return apurva;
}
```

The vidhi is a function definition. The agent's desire is the trigger condition. The ritual procedure is the function body. The apurva is the return value — a deferred promise of future execution.

### Austin's Speech Acts, Anticipated

J.L. Austin, in *How to Do Things with Words* (1962), distinguished between:

- **Constative utterances**: statements that describe the world (can be true or false)
- **Performative utterances**: statements that *do* something by being said ("I hereby pronounce you married")

Mimamsa made exactly this distinction 2,000 years earlier:

| Mimamsa Category | Austin's Category | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| विधि (vidhi) — injunction | Performative / Illocutionary act | Does not describe; it commands and thereby creates an obligation |
| अर्थवाद (arthavāda) — description | Constative | Describes or praises; can be true or false |

For Mimamsa, the *primary* function of Vedic language is performative. The Vedas are not a description of the world. They are a program for acting in the world. They are not true or false — they are *effective or ineffective*. A vidhi does not assert a fact; it creates a duty. It brings something into being — an obligation, an apurva — by the very act of utterance.

This is language as **executable code**. A Vedic mantra, correctly recited in the correct ritual context, does not merely describe a transformation — it *performs* one. The words are not about the action. The words **are** the action.

### The Compiler Matters

And this is why Mimamsa is so obsessive about correct interpretation. If the Vedas are code, then misreading them is a compilation error. A wrongly performed ritual does not produce a wrong result — it produces *no result*. The apurva is not generated. The function does not execute. Precision is not pedantry; it is the difference between a running program and a syntax error.

This is why Mimamsa developed the shadlinga (six marks of interpretation), the classification of Vedic sentences, the elaborate rules for resolving textual conflicts. These are not academic exercises. They are the **specification of the compiler** — the formal rules for correctly parsing Vedic source code into executable ritual.

### The Deepest Claim

Mimamsa's deepest claim, beneath all the technical apparatus, is this: **the universe responds to correctly structured action.** Not to belief. Not to prayer. Not to understanding. To *action*, performed according to precise specifications, using precise language, in precise sequence. The cosmos has an API, and the Vedas are the documentation.

This is either the most profound or the most superstitious claim in Indian philosophy, depending on your priors. But the intellectual framework Mimamsa built to support it — the hermeneutics, the philosophy of language, the theory of meaning, the epistemology, the analysis of normative statements — is extraordinary regardless of whether you accept the metaphysics.

## Historical Impact

Mimamsa's influence extends far beyond its own school:

| Domain | Mimamsa's Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Vedanta** | Shankara, Ramanuja, and Madhva all use Mimamsa hermeneutics. The shadlinga is their primary interpretive tool. |
| **Hindu law** | Dharmashastra interpretation relies on Mimamsa principles. Rules of textual priority, conflict resolution, and interpretive maxims are all Mimamsa-derived. |
| **Sanskrit grammar** | Mimamsa's analysis of sentence-meaning (vakya-artha) influenced and was influenced by Paninian grammar. |
| **Debate tradition** | Mimamsa's systematic approach to resolving apparent contradictions (विप्रतिपत्ति, vipratiapatti) shaped the structure of Indian philosophical debate. |
| **Buddhist-Hindu debates** | Kumarila was one of the most formidable opponents of Buddhist philosophy. His arguments against momentariness, against Buddhist epistemology, and for the reality of universals were landmarks. |
