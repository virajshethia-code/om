# The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali — 196 Threads on the Mastery of Mind

## Why This Text

The **योगसूत्र** (Yogasūtra) of Patanjali is the foundational text of Yoga as a philosophical system. Not as exercise — as a systematic technology for the cessation of mental disturbance. Everything that calls itself "yoga" in the modern world is, at most, one-eighth of what Patanjali describes. The other seven-eighths are about the architecture and engineering of consciousness.

You already know Samkhya's 25 tattvas — the map of reality from Purusha and Prakriti down through the gunas, the tanmatras, the indriyas. Patanjali takes that map and asks: *given that this is the structure of experience, how do you stop mistaking the structure for the self?*

The Yoga Sutras do not argue. They do not narrate. They instruct. Each sutra is a compressed instruction — a seed that expands with practice and commentary into an entire method.

## Context and Placement

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Author** | पतञ्जलि (Patañjali) — identity debated; possibly the same grammarian who wrote the Mahabhashya on Panini, possibly distinct |
| **Date** | ~200 BCE – 200 CE (scholarly consensus ranges widely; most place it ~2nd century BCE to 4th century CE) |
| **Sutra count** | 195 or 196 (depending on recension; the standard Vyasa-bhashya edition has 195) |
| **Structure** | 4 पाद (pāda, chapters/quarters) |
| **Language** | Classical Sanskrit, extreme compression |
| **Tradition** | One of the six orthodox darshanas (आस्तिक दर्शन) — the Yoga school |
| **Key commentary** | Vyasa-bhashya (~4th-5th century CE) — so authoritative it is sometimes considered inseparable from the sutras |

### The Name: Sutra

**सूत्र** (sūtra) — from the dhatu **सिव्** (siv, "to sew, to stitch")

| Derivative | Meaning | Construction |
|---|---|---|
| सूत्र (sūtra) | thread, aphorism | siv + tra (instrument suffix) — "that by which things are stitched together" |
| सूत्रकार (sūtrakāra) | author of sutras | sūtra + kāra (doer) |
| सूत्रधार (sūtradhāra) | the thread-holder, director | sūtra + dhāra (holder) — the one who holds all threads together |

A sutra has specific characteristics defined in the tradition:

> अल्पाक्षरम् असन्दिग्धं सारवत् विश्वतोमुखम् ।
> अस्तोभम् अनवद्यं च सूत्रं सूत्रविदो विदुः ॥
>
> *alpākṣaram asandigdhaṃ sāravat viśvatomukham*
> *astobham anavadyaṃ ca sūtraṃ sūtravido viduḥ*
>
> "Of few syllables, unambiguous, pithy, universally applicable, without filler, and faultless — such is what knowers of sutras call a sutra."

This is why sutras are nearly unintelligible without commentary. They are designed to be **mnemonic anchors** for a teaching tradition, not standalone prose. Each sutra is a compressed file that requires a decompression algorithm — that algorithm is the guru's oral explanation, later crystallized as bhashya (commentary).

### The Four Padas

| Pada | Sanskrit | Meaning | Sutra Range | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | समाधिपाद (Samādhi Pāda) | "On Absorption" | 1.1 – 1.51 | Definition of yoga, types of samadhi, obstacles and their resolution |
| 2 | साधनापाद (Sādhana Pāda) | "On Practice" | 2.1 – 2.55 | Kriya yoga, the kleshas, the eight limbs (yama through pratyahara) |
| 3 | विभूतिपाद (Vibhūti Pāda) | "On Powers" | 3.1 – 3.56 | Samyama, the siddhis, higher knowledge |
| 4 | कैवल्यपाद (Kaivalya Pāda) | "On Liberation" | 4.1 – 4.34 | The nature of mind, karma, and final liberation |

The architecture is deliberate: Pada 1 defines what you are aiming at. Pada 2 gives you the method. Pada 3 shows what happens along the way (and why not to get attached to it). Pada 4 explains what liberation actually means.

## Pada 1 — समाधिपाद (Samādhi Pāda): On Absorption

### The Opening: Definition of Yoga

The first four sutras are the entire system in compressed form. Everything else in all four padas is elaboration.

---

#### Sutra 1.1 — The Announcement

> **अथ योगानुशासनम् ॥ १.१ ॥**
> *atha yogānuśāsanam*
> "Now, the teaching of yoga."

| Word | Root | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **अथ** (atha) | — (particle) | Indeclinable | "now" — marks an auspicious beginning; implies prerequisite preparation is complete |
| **योग** (yoga) | √युज् (yuj, to yoke) | Masculine noun | yoga — the discipline being taught |
| **अनुशासनम्** (anuśāsanam) | अनु (anu, "following") + √शास् (śās, "to instruct, to govern") | Neuter noun, nominative singular | "instruction, teaching, discipline" — lit. "instruction that follows [a tradition]" |

The word **अथ** (atha) is not filler. In the sutra tradition, it signals: (1) an auspicious commencement, (2) that the student has already completed prerequisite study, and (3) that what follows is a systematic exposition. Vyasa's commentary says this "now" implies the student already has viveka (discrimination) and vairagya (dispassion) — you don't begin yoga on a whim.

---

#### Sutra 1.2 — The Definition

> **योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः ॥ १.२ ॥**
> *yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ*
> "Yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind."

This is the single most important sentence in the Yoga Sutras. Every other sutra is either preparation for, elaboration of, or consequence of this definition.

**Full compound deconstruction:**

| Word | Root / Base | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **योगः** (yogaḥ) | √युज् (yuj, "to yoke, unite, concentrate") | Masculine noun, nominative singular | "yoga" — here: the state of unified concentration |
| **चित्त** (citta) | √चित् (cit, "to perceive, to be conscious, to think") | Past participle used as noun | "the mind-field, consciousness, the thinking substance" — encompasses buddhi, ahamkara, and manas |
| **वृत्ति** (vṛtti) | √वृत् (vṛt, "to turn, to revolve, to exist in a mode") | Feminine noun | "turning, mode of activity, fluctuation, wave" — the patterns or movements of mind |
| **निरोधः** (nirodhaḥ) | नि (ni, "down, into") + √रुध् (rudh, "to obstruct, to arrest, to contain") | Masculine noun, nominative singular | "cessation, restraint, dissolution" — not suppression but complete stilling |

The compound **चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः** is a **तत्पुरुष** (tatpuruṣa) compound: "nirodha of the vrittis of chitta" — cessation of the turnings of the mind-stuff.

**What each term means precisely:**

**चित्त (citta)** is not simply "mind" in the English sense. It is the entire inner instrument (**अन्तःकरण**, antaḥkaraṇa) — the apparatus through which consciousness experiences. In Samkhya-Yoga terms, chitta encompasses:
- **बुद्धि** (buddhi) — the intellect, the determinative faculty
- **अहंकार** (ahaṃkāra) — the I-maker, the sense of separate selfhood
- **मनस्** (manas) — the sensory mind, the processing of sense data

**वृत्ति (vṛtti)** — from √वृत् (vṛt), "to turn." A vritti is a turning, a wave, a pattern of activity in the chitta. Think of chitta as a lake. Vrittis are the waves and ripples. When the lake is still, you see the bottom clearly — that is yoga. When it is turbulent, all you see are distortions — that is ordinary experience.

**निरोध (nirodha)** — this is not violent suppression. The prefix नि (ni) + √रुध् (rudh) gives "to contain, to hold back, to dissolve." Nirodha is a state where the vrittis subside — not because they are forced down but because the conditions producing them have been addressed. The difference matters: suppression creates latent impressions (samskaras) that reassert themselves. Nirodha dissolves the root cause.

---

#### Sutra 1.3 — The Result

> **तदा द्रष्टुः स्वरूपेऽवस्थानम् ॥ १.३ ॥**
> *tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe'vasthānam*
> "Then the seer abides in its own nature."

| Word | Root | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **तदा** (tadā) | तद् (tad, "that") | Indeclinable (correlative adverb) | "then" — when nirodha is achieved |
| **द्रष्टुः** (draṣṭuḥ) | √दृश् (dṛś, "to see") | Genitive singular of draṣṭṛ (the seer) | "of the seer" — i.e., Purusha, pure consciousness |
| **स्वरूपे** (svarūpe) | स्व (sva, "own") + रूप (rūpa, "form, nature") | Locative singular | "in [its] own form/nature" |
| **अवस्थानम्** (avasthānam) | अव (ava, "down") + √स्था (sthā, "to stand, to abide") | Neuter noun, nominative | "abiding, resting, establishment" |

When the vrittis cease, what remains? Not blankness — the seer. Purusha, which was always there, now recognizes itself without the distorting mirror of chitta. This is liberation in Samkhya-Yoga terms: not gaining something new but recognizing what was never lost.

---

#### Sutra 1.4 — The Alternative

> **वृत्तिसारूप्यमितरत्र ॥ १.४ ॥**
> *vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra*
> "Otherwise, [there is] identification with the fluctuations."

| Word | Root | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **वृत्ति** (vṛtti) | √वृत् (vṛt) | — | fluctuations (as above) |
| **सारूप्यम्** (sārūpyam) | स (sa, "with") + रूप (rūpa, "form") + य (ya, abstract suffix) | Neuter noun | "having the same form as, identification with, conformity to" |
| **इतरत्र** (itaratra) | इतर (itara, "other, otherwise") + त्र (tra, locative suffix) | Indeclinable | "at other times, otherwise" |

This is the diagnosis. When you are not in yoga (not in nirodha), you take yourself to be your thoughts. Purusha — pure awareness — mistakes itself for the shapes appearing in chitta. This is **अविद्या** (avidyā, ignorance), the fundamental misidentification. You think you *are* the anger, the anxiety, the ambition. You are actually the one watching them.

---

### The Five Vrittis

#### Sutra 1.5-1.11 — The Taxonomy of Mental Activity

> **वृत्तयः पञ्चतय्यः क्लिष्टाक्लिष्टाः ॥ १.५ ॥**
> *vṛttayaḥ pañcatayyaḥ kliṣṭākliṣṭāḥ*
> "The vrittis are five-fold, [either] afflicted or non-afflicted."

| Type of Vritti | Sanskrit | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Right knowledge | **प्रमाण** (pramāṇa) | प्र (pra) + √मा (mā, "to measure") | Valid cognition — perception, inference, testimony |
| 2. Wrong knowledge | **विपर्यय** (viparyaya) | वि (vi, "reversed") + परि (pari) + √इ (i, "to go") | Misconception — taking something for what it is not |
| 3. Imagination | **विकल्प** (vikalpa) | वि (vi) + √कॢप् (kḷp, "to be suited, to imagine") | Verbal construction without corresponding reality |
| 4. Sleep | **निद्रा** (nidrā) | नि (ni) + √द्रा (drā, "to sleep") | The vritti of absence — the mind resting on the experience of nothing |
| 5. Memory | **स्मृति** (smṛti) | √स्मृ (smṛ, "to remember") | Retention of past experience |

The taxonomy is exhaustive. Patanjali claims that every single mental event you have ever had falls into one of these five categories. Note that sleep is included — even the experience of unconsciousness is a vritti. And note that *right* knowledge is also a vritti. Even correct perception is a fluctuation of mind that must eventually be stilled. This is radical: yoga does not merely stop error. It stops all mental movement, including accurate thought.

Each vritti can be **क्लिष्ट** (kliṣṭa, "afflicted" — producing suffering, rooted in the kleshas) or **अक्लिष्ट** (akliṣṭa, "unafflicted" — not immediately causing suffering, potentially useful on the path). The same type of vritti — say, memory — can be afflicted (obsessive rumination) or unafflicted (remembering a teaching that clarifies understanding).

### Abhyasa and Vairagya — The Two Instruments

#### Sutra 1.12 — The Method

> **अभ्यासवैराग्याभ्यां तन्निरोधः ॥ १.१२ ॥**
> *abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṃ tan-nirodhaḥ*
> "By practice and dispassion, that cessation [is achieved]."

| Word | Root | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **अभ्यास** (abhyāsa) | अभि (abhi, "towards") + √अस् (as, "to throw, to cast") | Masculine noun | "repeated application, sustained effort, practice" — lit. "throwing oneself at it again and again" |
| **वैराग्य** (vairāgya) | वि (vi, "without") + राग (rāga, "coloring, passion, attachment") + य (ya) | Neuter noun | "dispassion, non-attachment" — from √रञ्ज् (rañj, "to be colored/dyed") |
| **आभ्याम्** (ābhyāṃ) | — | Instrumental dual | "by these two" |
| **तत्** (tat) | तद् (tad) | — | "that" (referring to nirodha) |
| **निरोधः** (nirodhaḥ) | — | — | cessation (as above) |

These are the two wings of the bird. Practice without detachment becomes obsession. Detachment without practice becomes passivity. Together, they produce nirodha.

**अभ्यास** (abhyāsa) is defined in 1.13-14:

> **तत्र स्थितौ यत्नोऽभ्यासः ॥ १.१३ ॥**
> *tatra sthitau yatno'bhyāsaḥ*
> "Of these, practice is the effort toward steadiness."

> **स तु दीर्घकालनैरन्तर्यसत्कारासेवितो दृढभूमिः ॥ १.१४ ॥**
> *sa tu dīrgha-kāla-nairantarya-satkārāsevito dṛḍhabhūmiḥ*
> "And that [practice] becomes firmly grounded when attended to for a long time, without interruption, and with devotion."

Three conditions for practice to take root: **दीर्घकाल** (dīrgha-kāla, "a long time"), **नैरन्तर्य** (nairantarya, "without interruption"), **सत्कार** (satkāra, "with reverence, with earnestness"). There are no shortcuts.

**वैराग्य** (vairāgya) is defined in 1.15:

> **दृष्टानुश्रविकविषयवितृष्णस्य वशीकारसंज्ञा वैराग्यम् ॥ १.१५ ॥**
> *dṛṣṭānuśravika-viṣaya-vitṛṣṇasya vaśīkāra-saṃjñā vairāgyam*
> "Dispassion is the consciousness of mastery in one who is free from craving for objects seen or heard about."

Vairagya is not indifference. It is not emotional flatness. It is **vitrishna** (वितृष्ण, "free from thirst") — the absence of craving. The word **वशीकार** (vaśīkāra, "mastery, having brought under control") indicates that the mind has been trained, not numbed. You can engage with objects without being pulled by them.

### Ishvara Pranidhana

#### Sutra 1.23 — The Devotional Path

> **ईश्वरप्रणिधानाद्वा ॥ १.२३ ॥**
> *īśvara-praṇidhānād vā*
> "Or, by devotion to Ishvara."

| Word | Root | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ईश्वर** (īśvara) | √ईश् (īś, "to rule, to be master") + वर (vara, suffix of capacity) | Masculine noun | "the Lord" — a special Purusha, untouched by affliction |
| **प्रणिधान** (praṇidhāna) | प्र (pra, "forth") + नि (ni, "down") + √धा (dhā, "to place") | Neuter noun | "placing down before, surrendering, total dedication" |
| **वा** (vā) | — | Indeclinable | "or" — indicating an alternative path |

This is where Yoga diverges from Samkhya. Samkhya is nir-ishvara (without God). Yoga introduces Ishvara — not as a creator deity but as a special kind of Purusha:

#### Sutra 1.24 — Who is Ishvara?

> **क्लेशकर्मविपाकाशयैरपरामृष्टः पुरुषविशेष ईश्वरः ॥ १.२४ ॥**
> *kleśa-karma-vipāka-āśayair aparāmṛṣṭaḥ puruṣa-viśeṣa īśvaraḥ*
> "Ishvara is a special Purusha, untouched by afflictions, actions, results, or latent impressions."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **क्लेश** (kleśa) | √क्लिश् (kliś, "to afflict") | the five afflictions (avidya etc.) |
| **कर्म** (karma) | √कृ (kṛ, "to do") | action and its binding force |
| **विपाक** (vipāka) | वि + √पच् (pac, "to cook, ripen") | the ripening of karmic fruit |
| **आशय** (āśaya) | आ + √शी (śī, "to rest, to lie") | latent impressions, the reservoir of samskaras |
| **अपरामृष्ट** (aparāmṛṣṭa) | अ (not) + परा (parā) + √मृश् (mṛś, "to touch") | untouched, unaffected |
| **पुरुषविशेष** (puruṣa-viśeṣa) | puruṣa + विशेष (viśeṣa, "distinction") | a distinct, special Purusha |

Ishvara is not God in the Abrahamic sense. There is no creation ex nihilo, no moral judgment, no personal relationship in the devotional sense. Ishvara is simply a Purusha who was *never* entangled with Prakriti. Regular purushas get identified with their vrittis. Ishvara never did. Ishvara is the existence proof that liberation is possible.

#### Sutra 1.27 — Ishvara's Expression

> **तस्य वाचकः प्रणवः ॥ १.२७ ॥**
> *tasya vācakaḥ praṇavaḥ*
> "The word designating Ishvara is Pranava (OM)."

**प्रणव** (praṇava) — from प्र (pra) + √णु (ṇu, "to praise, to sound") — "that which sounds forth, the primordial vibration." This connects the Yoga Sutras directly to the Mandukya Upanishad's teaching on OM as the sound-form of all states of consciousness.

### Stages of Samadhi (Pada 1)

Patanjali distinguishes samadhi into progressive stages:

| Stage | Sanskrit | Meaning | Object |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Savitarka** | सवितर्क (sa-vitarka) | "with gross thought" | Concentration on gross objects with verbal/conceptual overlay |
| **Nirvitarka** | निर्वितर्क (nir-vitarka) | "beyond gross thought" | Object shines without word or concept — direct apprehension |
| **Savichara** | सविचार (sa-vicāra) | "with subtle inquiry" | Concentration on subtle objects (tanmatras, mind itself) |
| **Nirvichara** | निर्विचार (nir-vicāra) | "beyond subtle inquiry" | Subtle object experienced without conceptual processing |
| **Sananda** | सानन्द (sa-ānanda) | "with bliss" | Object dissolves, only bliss of sattva remains |
| **Sasmita** | सास्मिता (sa-asmitā) | "with I-am-ness" | Only bare sense of self remains — the subtlest identification |

All of the above are **सबीज समाधि** (sabīja samādhi, "samadhi with seed") — they still rest on some object or trace. The culmination is:

**निर्बीज समाधि** (nirbīja samādhi, "seedless samadhi") — no object, no trace, no seed from which new vrittis can sprout. This is described in 1.51:

> **तस्यापि निरोधे सर्वनिरोधान्निर्बीजः समाधिः ॥ १.५१ ॥**
> *tasyāpi nirodhe sarva-nirodhān nirbījaḥ samādhiḥ*
> "When even that [subtlest impression] is restrained, from the restraint of everything, [there arises] seedless samadhi."

## Pada 2 — साधनापाद (Sādhana Pāda): On Practice

### Kriya Yoga — Yoga of Action

#### Sutra 2.1 — The Practical Starting Point

> **तपःस्वाध्यायेश्वरप्रणिधानानि क्रियायोगः ॥ २.१ ॥**
> *tapaḥ-svādhyāya-īśvara-praṇidhānāni kriyā-yogaḥ*
> "Austerity, self-study, and surrender to Ishvara constitute Kriya Yoga."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **तपस्** (tapas) | √तप् (tap, "to heat, to burn") | austerity, discipline, the fire of committed effort |
| **स्वाध्याय** (svādhyāya) | स्व (sva, "self") + अधि (adhi, "over") + √इ (i, "to go") | self-study — both study of scriptures and examination of one's own mind |
| **ईश्वरप्रणिधान** (īśvara-praṇidhāna) | — | surrender to Ishvara (as defined in Pada 1) |
| **क्रिया** (kriyā) | √कृ (kṛ, "to do") | action, practice |

Kriya Yoga is the *preliminary* practice — what you do before you are ready for the full eight limbs. It addresses the three levels at which a practitioner typically operates: body (tapas — disciplining the physical), mind (svadhyaya — study and self-reflection), and ego (ishvara pranidhana — surrendering the claim of doership).

---

### The Five Kleshas — Afflictions

#### Sutra 2.3 — The Root Causes of Suffering

> **अविद्यास्मितारागद्वेषाभिनिवेशाः क्लेशाः ॥ २.३ ॥**
> *avidyā-asmitā-rāga-dveṣa-abhiniveśāḥ kleśāḥ*
> "The afflictions are: ignorance, I-am-ness, attachment, aversion, and clinging to life."

| Klesha | Sanskrit | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Ignorance | **अविद्या** (avidyā) | अ (not) + √विद् (vid, "to know") | The foundational affliction — mistaking the non-eternal for eternal, the impure for pure, suffering for pleasure, the not-self for self (2.5) |
| 2. Egoism | **अस्मिता** (asmitā) | अस्मि (asmi, "I am") + ता (tā, abstract suffix) | "I-am-ness" — the confusion of Purusha (the seer) with the instrument of seeing (buddhi). Not ego in the Freudian sense but the *structural* error of identifying awareness with its vehicle |
| 3. Attachment | **राग** (rāga) | √रञ्ज् (rañj, "to be colored, dyed") | Craving for what has previously given pleasure — the mind is "dyed" by pleasant experience and pulls toward repetition |
| 4. Aversion | **द्वेष** (dveṣa) | √द्विष् (dviṣ, "to hate") | Repulsion from what has previously caused pain — the mirror image of raga |
| 5. Fear of death | **अभिनिवेश** (abhiniveśa) | अभि (abhi, "toward") + नि (ni, "into") + √विश् (viś, "to enter") | Clinging to embodied existence — lit. "entering deeply into." Even the learned have this instinct (2.9) |

#### Sutra 2.4 — The Structure of Ignorance

> **अविद्या क्षेत्रमुत्तरेषां प्रसुप्ततनुविच्छिन्नोदाराणाम् ॥ २.४ ॥**
> *avidyā kṣetram uttareṣāṃ prasupta-tanu-vicchinna-udārāṇām*
> "Ignorance is the field for the others, [which may be] dormant, attenuated, intermittent, or fully active."

Avidya is not one klesha among five — it is the soil from which the other four grow. And the kleshas exist in four states: **प्रसुप्त** (prasupta, dormant — a seed not yet sprouted), **तनु** (tanu, thin/attenuated — weakened by practice), **विच्छिन्न** (vicchinna, interrupted — alternating between active and dormant), **उदार** (udāra, fully active — in full operation). This is a remarkably precise clinical model. Modern cognitive science would recognize these as analogous to latent biases, weakened biases, intermittently triggered responses, and active cognitive distortions.

---

### Ashtanga Yoga — The Eight Limbs

#### Sutra 2.29 — The Architecture

> **यमनियमासनप्राणायामप्रत्याहारधारणाध्यानसमाधयोऽष्टावङ्गानि ॥ २.२९ ॥**
> *yama-niyama-āsana-prāṇāyāma-pratyāhāra-dhāraṇā-dhyāna-samādhayo'ṣṭāv aṅgāni*
> "The eight limbs are: restraint, observance, posture, breath control, sense withdrawal, concentration, meditation, and absorption."

The word **अङ्ग** (aṅga, "limb") is precise. These are limbs, not steps. A tree grows all its limbs together, though some emerge before others. The eight are practiced in relation, not in strict linear sequence — though there is a general progression from the outer (social conduct) to the inner (consciousness itself).

---

#### Limb 1: यम (Yama) — Restraints

**यम** (yama) — from √यम् (yam, "to restrain, to control") — the things you *do not do*.

| Yama | Sanskrit | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-violence | **अहिंसा** (ahiṃsā) | अ (not) + √हिंस् (hiṃs, "to harm") | Non-injury in thought, word, and deed — the foundational yama |
| Truthfulness | **सत्य** (satya) | √सत् (sat, "being, truth") + य (ya) | Speech and thought aligned with reality |
| Non-stealing | **अस्तेय** (asteya) | अ (not) + √स्तेय (steya, from √स्तेन/stena, "to steal") | Not taking what is not freely given — includes time, attention, credit |
| Continence | **ब्रह्मचर्य** (brahmacarya) | ब्रह्म (brahma, "the sacred") + √चर् (car, "to move/walk") | "Walking in Brahman" — conservation of vital energy; traditionally celibacy, but more broadly: non-dissipation |
| Non-possessiveness | **अपरिग्रह** (aparigraha) | अ (not) + परि (pari, "around") + √ग्रह् (grah, "to grasp") | Not grasping, not accumulating beyond need |

Patanjali then describes the powers that come from each yama being perfected. For example (2.35): when ahimsa is perfected, hostility ceases in your presence. These are not metaphorical claims in the original context.

---

#### Limb 2: नियम (Niyama) — Observances

**नियम** (niyama) — नि (ni, "into") + √यम् (yam, "to restrain") — the things you *actively do*.

| Niyama | Sanskrit | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cleanliness | **शौच** (śauca) | √शुच् (śuc, "to be pure, to shine") | Purity — external (body) and internal (mind) |
| Contentment | **सन्तोष** (santoṣa) | सम् (sam) + √तुष् (tuṣ, "to be satisfied") | Acceptance of what is — not resignation but the absence of craving for what is not |
| Austerity | **तपस्** (tapas) | √तप् (tap, "to heat") | Discipline, the willingness to endure discomfort for growth |
| Self-study | **स्वाध्याय** (svādhyāya) | स्व + अध्याय | Study of self and scripture (as above in Kriya Yoga) |
| Surrender | **ईश्वरप्रणिधान** (īśvara-praṇidhāna) | — | Devotion to Ishvara (as above) |

Note that the last three niyamas are identical to the three components of Kriya Yoga (2.1). The system is self-referential — kriya yoga is both a standalone preliminary practice and embedded within the full eight-limb structure.

---

#### Limb 3: आसन (Āsana) — Posture

**आसन** (āsana) — from √आस् (ās, "to sit") — "a seat, a sitting"

#### Sutra 2.46

> **स्थिरसुखमासनम् ॥ २.४६ ॥**
> *sthira-sukham āsanam*
> "Posture [should be] steady and comfortable."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **स्थिर** (sthira) | √स्था (sthā, "to stand") | steady, firm, stable |
| **सुख** (sukha) | सु (su, "good") + ख (kha, "space, axle-hole") | comfortable, easeful — lit. "good space" (originally: an axle that fits well) |

That is it. The entire instruction on asana. Two words: steady and comfortable. The 84 or 840,000 asanas of later Hatha Yoga are not Patanjali's concern. For him, asana is the *prerequisite* for the remaining limbs — you need a body that can sit still, without pain or restlessness, for extended periods. The body is not the object of yoga; it is the platform.

#### Sutra 2.47

> **प्रयत्नशैथिल्यानन्तसमापत्तिभ्याम् ॥ २.४७ ॥**
> *prayatna-śaithilya-ananta-samāpattibhyām*
> "By relaxation of effort and meditation on the infinite."

Asana is mastered not through force but through the *release* of effort. And the mind during asana should rest on the infinite (ananta — also the name of the cosmic serpent on which Vishnu reclines). The body settles when the mind expands.

---

#### Limb 4: प्राणायाम (Prāṇāyāma) — Breath Control

**प्राणायाम** (prāṇāyāma) — **प्राण** (prāṇa, from प्र + √अन्/an, "to breathe" — "the vital breath, life-force") + **आयाम** (āyāma, from आ + √यम्/yam, "to extend, stretch" — "extension, expansion")

So prānāyāma = "expansion of the life-force" — not merely breath control but the extension and regulation of the vital energy that breath carries.

Patanjali describes three components (2.50): **बाह्य** (bāhya, external — exhalation), **आभ्यन्तर** (ābhyantara, internal — inhalation), **स्तम्भ** (stambha, "suspension" — retention). Plus a fourth that transcends the other three.

---

#### Limb 5: प्रत्याहार (Pratyāhāra) — Sense Withdrawal

**प्रत्याहार** (pratyāhāra) — प्रति (prati, "back, against") + आ (ā) + √हृ (hṛ, "to seize, to carry") — "carrying back, withdrawing"

#### Sutra 2.54

> **स्वविषयासम्प्रयोगे चित्तस्वरूपानुकार इवेन्द्रियाणां प्रत्याहारः ॥ २.५४ ॥**
> *sva-viṣaya-asamprayoge citta-svarūpa-anukāra iva indriyāṇāṃ pratyāhāraḥ*
> "When the senses withdraw from their objects and conform, as it were, to the nature of the mind, that is pratyahara."

The senses normally flow outward toward objects. Pratyahara reverses this flow. The analogy in the tradition: when the queen bee stays in the hive, all the worker bees return. When chitta withdraws, the senses follow.

Pratyahara is the bridge between the outer limbs (yama through pranayama — which deal with behavior, body, and breath) and the inner limbs (dharana, dhyana, samadhi — which deal purely with consciousness). It is the hinge of the eight-limb system.

---

## Pada 3 — विभूतिपाद (Vibhūti Pāda): On Powers

### Dharana, Dhyana, Samadhi — The Inner Three

The first five limbs are preparation. Limbs 6, 7, and 8 are the inner practice — and they are stages of a single continuous process.

#### Limb 6: धारणा (Dhāraṇā) — Concentration

> **देशबन्धश्चित्तस्य धारणा ॥ ३.१ ॥**
> *deśa-bandhaś cittasya dhāraṇā*
> "Concentration is the binding of the mind to a single place."

**धारणा** (dhāraṇā) — from √धृ (dhṛ, "to hold, to bear") — "holding, fixing." The mind is held on one object — a point in the body, a mantra, an image, the breath. When it wanders, you bring it back. That is dharana. It is effortful.

---

#### Limb 7: ध्यान (Dhyāna) — Meditation

> **तत्र प्रत्ययैकतानता ध्यानम् ॥ ३.२ ॥**
> *tatra pratyaya-ekatānatā dhyānam*
> "Meditation is the continuous flow of cognition toward that [object]."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **प्रत्यय** (pratyaya) | प्रति + √इ (i, "to go") | cognition, the content of mental awareness |
| **एकतानता** (ekatānatā) | एक (eka, "one") + तान (tāna, from √तन्/tan, "to stretch") + ता | "single-stretchedness" — an unbroken, continuous flow |

Dharana is holding attention on an object with effort. Dhyana is when that holding becomes effortless — a continuous, unbroken stream. The difference is between gripping a rope and floating downstream. The object of concentration remains, but the struggle to maintain focus dissolves. This word, dhyana, traveled through Pali (jhāna), Chinese (chán), and Japanese (zen) to become "Zen."

---

#### Limb 8: समाधि (Samādhi) — Absorption

> **तदेवार्थमात्रनिर्भासं स्वरूपशून्यमिव समाधिः ॥ ३.३ ॥**
> *tad eva artha-mātra-nirbhāsaṃ svarūpa-śūnyam iva samādhiḥ*
> "When only the object shines forth, as if emptied of its own form — that is samadhi."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **अर्थमात्र** (artha-mātra) | artha ("object, meaning") + mātra ("only, mere") | only the object |
| **निर्भास** (nirbhāsa) | निर् (nir) + √भास् (bhās, "to shine") | shining forth |
| **स्वरूपशून्यम्** (svarūpa-śūnyam) | svarūpa ("own form") + śūnya ("empty") | "as if empty of own nature" — the meditator's self-awareness vanishes |

In dharana, there is: subject → effort → object. In dhyana, there is: subject → flow → object. In samadhi, there is: only object. The subject has vanished — not destroyed, but absorbed into the object of meditation. The distinction between knower and known collapses.

---

### Samyama — The Combined Practice

#### Sutra 3.4

> **त्रयमेकत्र संयमः ॥ ३.४ ॥**
> *trayam ekatra saṃyamaḥ*
> "The three together [on a single object] constitute samyama."

**संयम** (saṃyama) — सम् (sam, "together") + √यम् (yam, "to restrain, control") — "complete restraint, total integration."

Samyama = dharana + dhyana + samadhi applied to a single object. It is the master key of Pada 3. When you can perform samyama on any object, you gain complete knowledge of that object — its past, its nature, its hidden dimensions.

---

### The Siddhis — Powers and Their Danger

Patanjali then catalogs what happens when samyama is applied to various objects. This section (3.16-3.50) is the most exotic part of the Yoga Sutras:

| Samyama Object | Result (Siddhi) | Sutra |
|---|---|---|
| The three transformations (of chitta) | Knowledge of past and future | 3.16 |
| The distinction between word, meaning, and concept | Knowledge of all languages, animal cries | 3.17 |
| Latent impressions (samskaras) | Knowledge of previous births | 3.18 |
| The body's form | Invisibility | 3.21 |
| Strength (of elephant, etc.) | Gaining that strength | 3.24 |
| The sun | Knowledge of the world-system | 3.26 |
| The moon | Knowledge of the arrangement of stars | 3.27 |
| The navel center | Knowledge of the body's organization | 3.29 |
| The throat | Cessation of hunger and thirst | 3.30 |
| Karma | Knowledge of the time of death | 3.22 |
| Others' minds | Knowledge of other minds | 3.19 |

Whether these are literal, metaphorical, or phenomenological descriptions of meditative experience is debated across centuries of commentary. What is not debated is Patanjali's attitude toward them:

#### Sutra 3.37 — The Warning

> **ते समाधावुपसर्गा व्युत्थाने सिद्धयः ॥ ३.३७ ॥**
> *te samādhāv upasargā vyutthāne siddhayaḥ*
> "These are obstacles to samadhi, though they appear as powers in the outward-turned state."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **उपसर्ग** (upasarga) | उप (upa) + √सृज् (sṛj, "to release, emit") | obstacle, affliction — lit. "that which is thrown near" |
| **व्युत्थान** (vyutthāna) | वि + उत् + √स्था (sthā) | the outward-turned, ordinary state of mind — the opposite of samadhi |
| **सिद्धि** (siddhi) | √सिध् (sidh, "to succeed, to be accomplished") | attainment, power, perfection |

The siddhis are seductive precisely because they appear as accomplishments. But they are accomplishments of the mind — and the goal of yoga is to transcend mind. Attachment to siddhis is attachment to the instrument, not the destination. This is one of the most pragmatic warnings in all contemplative literature.

---

## Pada 4 — कैवल्यपाद (Kaivalya Pāda): On Liberation

### Kaivalya — Aloneness

**कैवल्य** (kaivalya) — from **केवल** (kevala, "alone, isolated, absolute") — "the state of being absolutely alone."

This is the Samkhya-Yoga term for liberation. It does not mean loneliness. It means Purusha recognizes itself as separate from Prakriti and all its modifications — including chitta, the gunas, the body, the senses, and the entire apparatus of experience. Kaivalya is the undoing of the original confusion: the seer stops identifying with the seen.

---

### Mind vs. Consciousness

Pada 4 makes a critical philosophical argument: the mind (chitta) is an *object*, not the subject. Consciousness is not a property of the brain or the mind — it is the light by which mind is known.

#### Sutra 4.19

> **न तत्स्वाभासं दृश्यत्वात् ॥ ४.१९ ॥**
> *na tat svābhāsaṃ dṛśyatvāt*
> "The mind is not self-luminous, because it is an object of perception."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **स्वाभास** (svābhāsa) | स्व (sva, "self") + आ + √भास् (bhās, "to shine") | self-illuminating |
| **दृश्यत्वात्** (dṛśyatvāt) | √दृश् (dṛś, "to see") + त्व (tva) + आत् | "because of being an object of perception" |

If you can observe your mind — notice your thoughts, watch your emotions, witness your memories — then the mind is an object, not the subject. The subject is that which observes. And that which observes is not itself an object of observation — it is Purusha. This argument has direct parallels in philosophy of consciousness (the "hard problem") and is structurally similar to Kant's transcendental unity of apperception.

#### Sutra 4.22

> **चितेरप्रतिसंक्रमायास्तदाकारापत्तौ स्वबुद्धिसंवेदनम् ॥ ४.२२ ॥**
> *citer apratisaṃkramāyās tad-ākāra-āpattau sva-buddhi-saṃvedanam*
> "When consciousness, which does not move, assumes the form [of the object], then there is knowledge of one's own cognition."

This is the mechanism: Purusha (consciousness) does not move or change. But chitta takes on forms (ākāra) — like a crystal taking on the color of whatever is placed near it. When chitta takes the form of an object, Purusha "sees" through chitta as through a lens. Liberation is recognizing that you are the Purusha, not the lens, not the forms.

---

### Dharma-Megha Samadhi — The Cloud of Dharma

#### Sutra 4.29

> **प्रसंख्याने'प्यकुसीदस्य सर्वथाविवेकख्यातेर्धर्ममेघः समाधिः ॥ ४.२९ ॥**
> *prasaṃkhyāne'py akusīdasya sarvathā viveka-khyāter dharma-meghaḥ samādhiḥ*
> "For one who is without self-interest even in the highest knowledge, and who has constant discriminative discernment, [there arises] the samadhi called dharma-megha."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **प्रसंख्यान** (prasaṃkhyāna) | प्र + सम् + √ख्या (khyā, "to tell, to know") | the highest meditation, the most elevated knowledge |
| **अकुसीद** (akusīda) | अ (not) + कुसीद (kusīda, "self-interest, usury") | without self-interest — even in one's own liberation |
| **विवेकख्याति** (viveka-khyāti) | viveka ("discrimination") + khyāti ("discernment, vision") | constant discriminative knowledge — the unbroken awareness of the difference between Purusha and Prakriti |
| **धर्ममेघ** (dharma-megha) | dharma ("truth, righteousness, order") + megha ("cloud") | "cloud of dharma" |

Dharma-megha samadhi is the final stage. The metaphor: as a cloud rains without effort or intention, dharma (truth, virtue, cosmic order) rains down upon the consciousness that has achieved this state. There is no more striving. There is no more seeking. Even the desire for liberation has dissolved — because desire itself has dissolved. What remains is a kind of spontaneous, effortless alignment with reality.

#### Sutra 4.34 — The Final Sutra

> **पुरुषार्थशून्यानां गुणानां प्रतिप्रसवः कैवल्यं स्वरूपप्रतिष्ठा वा चितिशक्तिरिति ॥ ४.३४ ॥**
> *puruṣa-artha-śūnyānāṃ guṇānāṃ pratiprasavaḥ kaivalyaṃ svarūpa-pratiṣṭhā vā citi-śaktir iti*
> "When the gunas, having no further purpose for Purusha, return to their source — that is kaivalya, the establishment in one's own nature, the power of consciousness [standing alone]. Thus."

| Word | Root | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **पुरुषार्थशून्य** (puruṣārtha-śūnya) | puruṣa + artha ("purpose") + śūnya ("empty") | "empty of purpose for Purusha" — the gunas have nothing left to do |
| **प्रतिप्रसव** (pratiprasava) | प्रति (prati, "back") + प्र (pra) + √सू (sū, "to give birth") | "re-absorption, involution" — evolution in reverse; Prakriti's products collapse back into their source |
| **स्वरूपप्रतिष्ठा** (svarūpa-pratiṣṭhā) | svarūpa + pratiṣṭhā (from प्रति + √स्था/sthā, "to stand firm") | "establishment in one's own nature" |
| **चितिशक्ति** (citi-śakti) | चिति (citi, from √चित्/cit, "pure consciousness") + शक्ति (śakti, "power") | "the power of pure consciousness" |
| **इति** (iti) | — | "thus" — the concluding word of the entire text |

This is the mirror image of sutra 1.3 (तदा द्रष्टुः स्वरूपे'वस्थानम्). The text ends where it began: the seer abides in its own nature. But now the full path has been walked. The gunas — sattva, rajas, tamas — which had evolved an entire universe of experience for the sake of Purusha's liberation, have served their purpose. Like actors leaving a stage after the play is over, they dissolve back into the unmanifest equilibrium of Prakriti. Purusha stands alone. Not lonely — complete.

## Commentarial Tradition

| Commentary | Author | Date | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **योगभाष्य** (Yogabhāṣya) | Vyasa (not Mahabharata Vyasa) | ~4th-5th century CE | The foundational commentary; so integrated with the sutras that some scholars treat them as one text |
| **तत्त्ववैशारदी** (Tattvavaiśāradī) | Vachaspati Mishra | ~9th century CE | Sub-commentary on Vyasa; Advaita-leaning interpretation |
| **राजमार्तण्ड** (Rājamārtaṇḍa) | Bhoja Raja | ~11th century CE | Independent commentary; gives a theistic reading |
| **योगवार्त्तिक** (Yogavārttika) | Vijnanabhikshu | ~16th century CE | Attempts to reconcile Yoga with Vedanta |

Each commentary reads the same 196 sutras through a different philosophical lens — which is precisely why sutra literature works. The thread holds; the weaving differs.
